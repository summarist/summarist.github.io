Book 1

1:1 The Mas­ter said, ʼIs it not pleas­ant to learn with a con­stant per­se­ver­ance and ap­pli­ca­tion? ʼIs it not de­light­ful to have friends com­ing from dis­tant quar­ters?ʼ ʼIs he not a man of com­plete vir­tue, who feels no dis­com­po­sure though men may take no note of him?ʼ

1:2 The phi­los­o­pher Yu said, ʼThey are few who, be­ing fil­ial and fra­ter­nal, are fond of offending a­gainst their su­pe­ri­ors. There have been none, who, not lik­ing to of­fend a­gainst their su­pe­ri­ors, have been fond of stir­ring up con­fu­sion. ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man bends his at­ten­tion to what is rad­i­cal. That be­ing es­tab­lished, all prac­ti­cal cours­es nat­u­ral­ly grow up. Fil­ial pi­e­ty and fra­ter­nal sub­mis­sion!-- are they not the root of all be­nev­o­lent ac­tions?ʼ

1:3 The Mas­ter said, ʼFine words and an in­sin­u­at­ing ap­pear­ance are sel­dom as­so­ci­at­ed with true vir­tue.ʼ

1:4 The phi­los­o­pher Tsang said, ʼI dai­ly ex­am­ine my­self on three points:-- wheth­er, in transacting busi­ness for oth­ers, I may have been not faith­ful;-- wheth­er, in in­ter­course with friends, I may have been not sin­cere;-- wheth­er I may have not mas­tered and prac­tised the in­struc­tions of my teach­er.ʼ

1:5 The Mas­ter said, To rule a coun­try of a thou­sand char­i­ots, there must be rev­er­ent at­ten­tion to busi­ness, and sin­cer­i­ty; e­con­o­my in ex­pend­i­ture, and love for men; and the em­ploy­ment of the peo­ple at the prop­er sea­sons.ʼ 6 The Mas­ter said, ʼA youth, when at home, should be fil­ial, and, a­broad, re­spect­ful to his el­ders. He should be ear­nest and truth­ful. He should o­ver­flow in love to all, and cul­ti­vate the friend­ship of the good. When he has time and op­por­tu­ni­ty, af­ter the per­for­mance of these things, he should em­ploy them in po­lite stud­ies.ʼ 7 Tsze-hsia said, ʼIf a man with­draws his mind from the love of beau­ty, and applies it as sin­cere­ly to the love of the vir­tu­ous; if, in serv­ing his par­ents, he can ex­ert his ut­most strength; if, in serv­ing his prince, he can de­vote his life; if, in his in­ter­course with his friends, his words are sin­cere:-- al­though men say that he has not learn­ed, I will cer­tain­ly say that he has.ʼ 8 The Mas­ter said, ʼIf the schol­ar be not gra­ve, he will not call forth any ven­er­a­tion, and his learn­ing will not be sol­id. ʼHold faith­ful­ness and sin­cer­i­ty as first prin­ci­ples. ʼHave no friends not e­qual to your­self. ʼWhen you have faults, do not fear to a­ban­don them.ʼ

1:9 The phi­los­o­pher Tsang said, ʼLet there be a care­ful at­ten­tion to per­form the fu­ner­al rites to par­ents, and let them be fol­lowed when long gone with the ceremonies of sac­ri­fice;-- then the vir­tue of the peo­ple will re­sume its prop­er ex­cel­lence.ʼ

1:10 Tsze-chʼin asked Tsze-kung, say­ing, ʼWhen our mas­ter co­mes to any coun­try, he does not fail to learn all a­bout its gov­ern­ment. Does he ask his in­for­ma­tion? or is it giv­en to him?ʼ Tsze-kung said, ʼOur mas­ter is be­nign, up­right, cour­te­ous, tem­per­ate, and com­plai­sant, and thus he gets his in­for­ma­tion. The mas­terʼs mode of asking in­for­ma­tion!-- is it not dif­fer­ent from that of oth­er men?ʼ 11 The Mas­ter said, ʼWhile a manʼs fa­ther is a­live, look at the bent of his will; when his fa­ther is dead, look at his con­duct. If for three years he does not al­ter from the way of his fa­ther, he may be called fil­ial.ʼ

1:12 The phi­los­o­pher Yu said, ʼIn practising the rules of pro­pri­e­ty, a nat­u­ral ease is to be prized. In the ways pre­scribed by the an­cient kings, this is the ex­cel­lent qual­i­ty, and in things small and great we fol­low them. ʼYet it is not to be ob­served in all cases. If one, know­ing how such ease should be prized, man­i­fests it, with­out reg­u­lat­ing it by the rules of pro­pri­e­ty, this like­wise is not to be done.ʼ 13 The phi­los­o­pher Yu said, ʼWhen a­gree­ments are made ac­cord­ing to what is right, what is spo­ken can be made good. When re­spect is shown ac­cord­ing to what is prop­er, one keeps far from shame and dis­grace. When the parties up­on whom a man leans are prop­er per­sons to be in­ti­mate with, he can make them his guides and mas­ters.ʼ 14 The Mas­ter said, ʼHe who aims to be a man of com­plete vir­tue in his food does not seek to grat­i­fy his ap­pe­tite, nor in his dwell­ing place does he seek the ap­pli­ances of ease; he is ear­nest in what he is do­ing, and care­ful in his speech; he fre­quents the com­pa­ny of men of prin­ci­ple that he may be rec­ti­fied:-- such a per­son may be said in­deed to love to learn.ʼ

1:15 Tsze-kung said, ʼWhat do you pro­nounce con­cern­ing the poor man who yet does not flat­ter, and the rich man who is not proud?ʼ The Mas­ter replied, ʼThey will do; but they are not e­qual to him, who, though poor, is yet cheer­ful, and to him, who, though rich, loves the rules of pro­pri­e­ty.ʼ Tsze-kung replied, ʼIt is said in the Book of Po­et­ry, "As you cut and then file, as you carve and then pol­ish."-- The mean­ing is the same, I ap­pre­hend, as that which you have just ex­pressed.ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼWith one like Tsʼze, I can be­gin to talk a­bout the odes. I told him one point, and he knew its prop­er se­quence.ʼ

1:16 The Mas­ter said, ʼI will not be af­flicted at menʼs not know­ing me; I will be af­flicted that I do not know men.ʼ

Book 2

2:1 The Mas­ter said, ʼHe who ex­er­cises gov­ern­ment by means of his vir­tue may be com­pared to the north po­lar star, which keeps its place and all the stars turn to­wards it.ʼ 2 The Mas­ter said, ʼIn the Book of Po­et­ry are three hun­dred pieces, but the de­sign of them all may be em­braced in one sen­tence-- "Having no de­praved thoughts."ʼ

2:3 The Mas­ter said, ʼIf the peo­ple be led by laws, and u­ni­form­i­ty sought to be giv­en them by pun­ish­ments, they will try to a­void the pun­ish­ment, but have no sense of shame. ʼIf they be led by vir­tue, and u­ni­form­i­ty sought to be giv­en them by the rules of pro­pri­e­ty, they will have the sense of shame, and more­o­ver will be­come good.ʼ

2:4 The Mas­ter said, ʼAt fif­teen, I had my mind bent on learn­ing. ʼAt thir­ty, I stood firm. ʼAt for­ty, I had no doubts. ʼAt fif­ty, I knew the de­crees of Heav­en. ʼAt six­ty, my ear was an o­be­di­ent or­gan for the re­cep­tion of truth. ʼAt sev­en­ty, I could fol­low what my heart de­sired, with­out transgressing what was right.ʼ

2:5 Mang I asked what fil­ial pi­e­ty was. The Mas­ter said, ʼIt is not be­ing dis­o­be­di­ent.ʼ Soon af­ter, as Fan Chʼih was driv­ing him, the Mas­ter told him, say­ing, ʼMang-sun asked me what fil­ial pi­e­ty was, and I an­swered him,-- "not be­ing dis­o­be­di­ent."ʼ Fan Chʼih said, ʼWhat did you mean?ʼ The Mas­ter replied, ʼThat par­ents, when a­live, be served ac­cord­ing to pro­pri­e­ty; that, when dead, they should be bur­ied ac­cord­ing to pro­pri­e­ty; and that they should be sacrificed to ac­cord­ing to pro­pri­e­ty.ʼ

2:6 Mang Wu asked what fil­ial pi­e­ty was. The Mas­ter said, ʼParents are anx­ious lest their chil­dren should be sick.ʼ

2:7 Tsze-yu asked what fil­ial pi­e­ty was. The Mas­ter said, ʼThe fil­ial pi­e­ty of now-a-days means the sup­port of oneʼs par­ents. But dogs and hors­es like­wise are a­ble to do some­thing in the way of sup­port;-- with­out rev­er­ence, what is there to dis­tin­guish the one sup­port giv­en from the oth­er?ʼ

2:8 Tsze-hsia asked what fil­ial pi­e­ty was. The Mas­ter said, ʼThe dif­fi­cul­ty is with the coun­te­nance. If, when their el­ders have any trou­ble­some af­fairs, the young take the toil of them, and if, when the young have wine and food, they set them be­fore their el­ders, is THIS to be con­sid­ered fil­ial pi­e­ty?ʼ

2:9 The Mas­ter said, ʼI have talked with Hui for a whole day, and he has not made any ob­jec­tion to any­thing I said;-- as if he were stu­pid. He has re­tired, and I have ex­am­ined his con­duct when a­way from me, and found him a­ble to il­lus­trate my teach­ings. Hui!-- He is not stu­pid.ʼ

2:10 The Mas­ter said, ʼSee what a man does. ʼMark his mo­tives. ʼExamine in what things he rests. ʼHow can a man con­ceal his char­ac­ter? How can a man con­ceal his char­ac­ter?ʼ

2:11 The Mas­ter said, ʼIf a man keeps cherishing his old knowl­edge, so as con­tin­u­al­ly to be ac­quir­ing new, he may be a teach­er of oth­ers.ʼ

2:12 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe ac­com­plished schol­ar is not a u­ten­sil.ʼ

2:13 Tsze-kung asked what con­sti­tut­ed the su­pe­ri­or man. The Mas­ter said, ʼHe acts be­fore he speaks, and af­ter­wards speaks ac­cord­ing to his ac­tions.ʼ

2:14 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man is cath­o­lic and no par­ti­san. The mean man is par­ti­san and not cath­o­lic.ʼ

2:15 The Mas­ter said, ʼLearning with­out thought is la­bour lost; thought with­out learn­ing is per­i­lous.ʼ

2:16 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe stud­y of strange doc­trines is in­ju­ri­ous in­deed!ʼ

2:17 The Mas­ter said, ʼYu, shall I teach you what knowl­edge is? When you know a thing, to hold that you know it; and when you do not know a thing, to al­low that you do not know it;-- this is knowl­edge.ʼ

2:18 Tsze-chang was learn­ing with a view to of­fi­cial e­mol­u­ment. The Mas­ter said, ʼHear much and put a­side the points of which you stand in doubt, while you speak cau­tious­ly at the same time of the oth­ers:-- then you will af­ford few oc­ca­sions for blame. See much and put a­side the things which seem per­i­lous, while you are cau­tious at the same time in car­ry­ing the oth­ers in­to prac­tice:-- then you will have few oc­ca­sions for re­pent­ance. When one gives few oc­ca­sions for blame in his words, and few oc­ca­sions for re­pent­ance in his con­duct, he is in the way to get e­mol­u­ment.ʼ

2:19 The Duke A­i asked, say­ing, ʼWhat should be done in or­der to se­cure the sub­mis­sion of the peo­ple?ʼ Confucius replied, ʼAdvance the up­right and set a­side the crook­ed, then the peo­ple will sub­mit. Ad­vance the crook­ed and set a­side the up­right, then the peo­ple will not sub­mit.ʼ

2:20 Chi Kʼang asked how to cause the peo­ple to rev­er­ence their rul­er, to be faith­ful to him, and to go on to nerve them­selves to vir­tue. The Mas­ter said, ʼLet him pre­side o­ver them with grav­i­ty;-- then they will rev­er­ence him. Let him be fil­ial and kind to all;-- then they will be faith­ful to him. Let him ad­vance the good and teach the in­com­pe­tent;-- then they will ea­ger­ly seek to be vir­tu­ous.ʼ

2:21 Some one ad­dressed Confucius, say­ing, ʼSir, why are you not en­gaged in the gov­ern­ment?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼWhat does the Shu-ching say of fil­ial pi­e­ty?-- "You are fil­ial, you dis­charge your broth­er­ly duties. These qualities are dis­played in gov­ern­ment." This then al­so con­sti­tutes the ex­er­cise of gov­ern­ment. Why must there be THAT-- mak­ing one be in the gov­ern­ment?ʼ

2:22 The Mas­ter said, ʼI do not know how a man with­out truthfulness is to get on. How can a large car­riage be made to go with­out the cross-bar for yok­ing the ox­en to, or a small car­riage with­out the ar­range­ment for yok­ing the hors­es?ʼ

2:23 Tsze-chang asked wheth­er the af­fairs of ten ages af­ter could be known. Confucius said, ʼThe Yin dyn­as­ty fol­lowed the reg­u­la­tions of the Hsia: where­in it took from or added to them may be known. The Chau dyn­as­ty has fol­lowed the reg­u­la­tions of Yin: where­in it took from or added to them may be known. Some oth­er may fol­low the Chau, but though it should be at the dis­tance of a hun­dred ages, its af­fairs may be known.ʼ

2:24 The Mas­ter said, ʼFor a man to sac­ri­fice to a spir­it which does not be­long to him is flat­ter­y. ʼTo see what is right and not to do it is want of cour­age.ʼ

Book 3

3:1 Confucius said of the head of the Chi fam­i­ly, who had eight rows of pan­to­mimes in his ar­e­a, ʼIf he can bear to do this, what may he not bear to do?ʼ

3:2 The three families used the YUNG ode, while the ves­sels were be­ing re­moved, at the con­clu­sion of the sac­ri­fice. The Mas­ter said, ʼ"Assisting are the prin­ces;-- the son of heav­en looks pro­found and gra­ve:"-- what ap­pli­ca­tion can these words have in the hall of the three families?ʼ

3:3 The Mas­ter said, ʼIf a man be with­out the vir­tues prop­er to hu­man­i­ty, what has he to do with the rites of pro­pri­e­ty? If a man be with­out the vir­tues prop­er to hu­man­i­ty, what has he to do with mu­sic?ʼ

3:4 Lin Fang asked what was the first thing to be at­tended to in ceremonies. The Mas­ter said, ʼA great ques­tion in­deed! ʼIn fes­tive ceremonies, it is bet­ter to be spar­ing than ex­trav­a­gant. In the ceremonies of mourn­ing, it is bet­ter that there be deep sor­row than a min­ute at­ten­tion to ob­ser­vances.ʼ

3:5 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe rude tribes of the east and north have their prin­ces, and are not like the States of our great land which are with­out them.ʼ

3:6 The chief of the Chi fam­i­ly was a­bout to sac­ri­fice to the Tʼai moun­tain. The Mas­ter said to Zan Yu, ʼCan you not save him from this?ʼ He an­swered, ʼI can­not.ʼ Confucius said, ʼAlas! will you say that the Tʼai moun­tain is not so dis­cern­ing as Lin Fang?ʼ

3:7 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe stu­dent of vir­tue has no con­ten­tions. If it be said he can­not a­void them, shall this be in ar­cher­y? But he bows com­plai­sant­ly to his com­pet­i­tors; thus he as­cends the hall, de­scends, and ex­acts the for­feit of drink­ing. In his con­ten­tion, he is still the Chun-tsze.ʼ

3:8 Tsze-hsia asked, say­ing, ʼWhat is the mean­ing of the pas­sage-- "The pret­ty dim­ples of her art­ful smile! The well- de­fined black and white of her eye! The plain ground for the colours?"ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼThe busi­ness of lay­ing on the colours fol­lows (the prep­a­ra­tion of) the plain ground.ʼ ʼCeremonies then are a sub­se­quent thing?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼIt is Shang who can bring out my mean­ing. Now I can be­gin to talk a­bout the odes with him.ʼ

3:9 The Mas­ter said, ʼI could de­scribe the ceremonies of the Hsia dyn­as­ty, but Chi can­not sufficiently at­test my words. I could de­scribe the ceremonies of the Yin dyn­as­ty, but Sung can­not sufficiently at­test my words. (They can­not do so) be­cause of the in­suf­fi­cien­cy of their re­cords and wise men. If those were suf­fi­cient, I could ad­duce them in sup­port of my words.ʼ

3:10 The Mas­ter said, ʼAt the great sac­ri­fice, af­ter the pouring out of the li­ba­tion, I have no wish to look on.ʼ

3:11 Some one asked the mean­ing of the great sac­ri­fice. The Mas­ter said, ʼI do not know. He who knew its mean­ing would find it as eas­y to gov­ern the king­dom as to look on this;-- point­ing to his palm.

3:12 He sacrificed to the dead, as if they were pres­ent. He sacrificed to the spir­its, as if the spir­its were pres­ent. The Mas­ter said, ʼI con­sid­er my not be­ing pres­ent at the sac­ri­fice, as if I did not sac­ri­fice.ʼ

3:13 Wang-sun Chia asked, say­ing, ʼWhat is the mean­ing of the say­ing, "It is bet­ter to pay court to the fur­nace than to the south-west cor­ner?"ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼNot so. He who of­fends a­gainst Heav­en has none to whom he can pray.ʼ

3:14 The Mas­ter said, ʼChau had the ad­van­tage of view­ing the two past dynasties. How com­plete and el­e­gant are its reg­u­la­tions! I fol­low Chau.ʼ

3:15 The Mas­ter, when he en­tered the grand tem­ple, asked a­bout eve­ry­thing. Some one said, ʼWho will say that the son of the man of Tsau knows the rules of pro­pri­e­ty! He has en­tered the grand tem­ple and asks a­bout eve­ry­thing.ʼ The Mas­ter heard the re­mark, and said, ʼThis is a rule of pro­pri­e­ty.ʼ

3:16 The Mas­ter said, ʼIn ar­cher­y it is not go­ing through the leath­er which is the prin­ci­pal thing;-- be­cause peo­pleʼs strength is not e­qual. This was the old way.ʼ

3:17 Tsze-kung wished to do a­way with the of­fer­ing of a sheep con­nect­ed with the in­au­gu­ra­tion of the first day of each month. The Mas­ter said, ʼTsʼze, you love the sheep; I love the cer­e­mo­ny.ʼ

3:18 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe full ob­serv­ance of the rules of pro­pri­e­ty in serv­ing oneʼs prince is ac­counted by peo­ple to be flat­ter­y.ʼ

3:19 The Duke Ting asked how a prince should em­ploy his min­is­ters, and how min­is­ters should serve their prince. Confucius replied, ʼA prince should em­ploy his min­is­ter ac­cord­ing to ac­cord­ing to the rules of pro­pri­e­ty; min­is­ters should serve their prince with faith­ful­ness.ʼ

3:20 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe Kwan Tsu is ex­pres­sive of en­joy­ment with­out be­ing li­cen­tious, and of grief with­out be­ing hurt­ful­ly ex­ces­sive.ʼ

3:21 The Duke A­i asked Tsai Wo a­bout the al­tars of the spir­its of the land. Tsai Wo replied, ʼThe Hsia sov­er­eign planted the pine tree a­bout them; the men of the Yin planted the cy­press; and the men of the Chau planted the chest­nut tree, mean­ing there­by to cause the peo­ple to be in awe.ʼ When the Mas­ter heard it, he said, ʼThings that are done, it is need­less to speak a­bout; things that have had their course, it is need­less to re­mon­strate a­bout; things that are past, it is need­less to blame.ʼ

3:22 The Mas­ter said, ʼSmall in­deed was the ca­pac­i­ty of Kwan Chung!ʼ Some one said, ʼWas Kwan Chung par­si­mo­ni­ous?ʼ ʼKwan,ʼ was the re­ply, ʼhad the San Kwei, and his of­fic­ers per­formed no dou­ble duties; how can he be con­sid­ered par­si­mo­ni­ous?ʼ ʼThen, did Kwan Chung know the rules of pro­pri­e­ty?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼThe prin­ces of States have a screen intercepting the view at their gates. Kwan had like­wise a screen at his gate. The prin­ces of States on any friend­ly meet­ing be­tween two of them, had a stand on which to place their in­verted cups. Kwan had al­so such a stand. If Kwan knew the rules of pro­pri­e­ty, who does not know them?ʼ

3:23 The Mas­ter instructing the grand mu­sic-master of Lu said, ʼHow to play mu­sic may be known. At the com­mence­ment of the piece, all the parts should sound to­geth­er. As it pro­ceeds, they should be in har­mo­ny while sev­er­al­ly dis­tinct and flow­ing with­out break, and thus on to the con­clu­sion.ʼ

3:24 The bor­der war­den at Yi re­quested to be in­tro­duced to the Mas­ter, say­ing, ʼWhen men of su­pe­ri­or vir­tue have come to this, I have nev­er been de­nied the priv­i­lege of see­ing them.ʼ The fol­low­ers of the sage in­tro­duced him, and when he came out from the in­ter­view, he said, ʼMy friends, why are you dis­tressed by your mas­terʼs loss of of­fice? The king­dom has long been with­out the prin­ci­ples of truth and right; Heav­en is go­ing to use your mas­ter as a bell with its wood­en tongue.ʼ

3:25 The Mas­ter said of the Shao that it was per­fect­ly beau­ti­ful and al­so per­fect­ly good. He said of the Wu that it was per­fect­ly beau­ti­ful but not per­fect­ly good.

3:26 The Mas­ter said, ʼHigh sta­tion filled with­out in­dul­gent gen­er­os­i­ty; ceremonies per­formed with­out rev­er­ence; mourn­ing con­ducted with­out sor­row;-- where­with should I con­tem­plate such ways?ʼ

Book 4

4:1 The Mas­ter said, ʼIt is vir­tu­ous man­ners which con­sti­tute the ex­cel­lence of a neigh­bor­hood. If a man in selecting a res­i­dence, do not fix on one where such pre­vail, how can he be wise?ʼ

4:2 The Mas­ter said, ʼThose who are with­out vir­tue can­not a­bide long ei­ther in a con­di­tion of pov­er­ty and hard­ship, or in a con­di­tion of en­joy­ment. The vir­tu­ous rest in vir­tue; the wise de­sire vir­tue.ʼ

4:3 The Mas­ter said, ʼIt is on­ly the (truly) vir­tu­ous man, who can love, or who can hate, oth­ers.ʼ

4:4 The Mas­ter said, ʼIf the will be set on vir­tue, there will be no prac­tice of wick­ed­ness.ʼ

4:5 The Mas­ter said, ʼRiches and honours are what men de­sire. If it can­not be ob­tained in the prop­er way, they should not be held. Pov­er­ty and meanness are what men dis­like. If it can­not be a­voided in the prop­er way, they should not be a­voided. ʼIf a su­pe­ri­or man a­ban­don vir­tue, how can he ful­fil the re­quire­ments of that name? ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man does not, e­ven for the space of a sin­gle meal, act con­tra­ry to vir­tue. In mo­ments of haste, he cleaves to it. In sea­sons of dan­ger, he cleaves to it.ʼ

4:6 The Mas­ter said, ʼI have not seen a per­son who loved vir­tue, or one who hat­ed what was not vir­tu­ous. He who loved vir­tue, would es­teem noth­ing a­bove it. He who hat­ed what is not vir­tu­ous, would prac­tise vir­tue in such a way that he would not al­low any­thing that is not vir­tu­ous to ap­proach his per­son. ʼIs any one a­ble for one day to ap­ply his strength to vir­tue? I have not seen the case in which his strength would be in­suf­fi­cient. ʼShould there pos­si­bly be any such case, I have not seen it.ʼ

4:7 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe faults of men are char­ac­ter­is­tic of the class to which they be­long. By ob­serv­ing a manʼs faults, it may be known that he is vir­tu­ous.ʼ

4:8 The Mas­ter said, ʼIf a man in the morn­ing hear the right way, he may die in the eve­ning with­out re­gret.ʼ

4:9 The Mas­ter said, ʼA schol­ar, whose mind is set on truth, and who is a­shamed of bad clothes and bad food, is not fit to be dis­coursed with.ʼ

4:10 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man, in the world, does not set his mind ei­ther for any­thing, or a­gainst any­thing; what is right he will fol­low.ʼ

4:11 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man thinks of vir­tue; the small man thinks of com­fort. The su­pe­ri­or man thinks of the sanc­tions of law; the small man thinks of fa­vours which he may re­ceive.ʼ

4:12 The Mas­ter said: ʼHe who acts with a con­stant view to his own ad­van­tage will be much mur­mured a­gainst.ʼ

4:13 The Mas­ter said, ʼIf a prince is a­ble to gov­ern his king­dom with the com­plai­sance prop­er to the rules of pro­pri­e­ty, what dif­fi­cul­ty will he have? If he can­not gov­ern it with that com­plai­sance, what has he to do with the rules of pro­pri­e­ty?ʼ

4:14 The Mas­ter said, ʼA man should say, I am not con­cerned that I have no place, I am con­cerned how I may fit my­self for one. I am not con­cerned that I am not known, I seek to be wor­thy to be known.ʼ

4:15 The Mas­ter said, ʼShan, my doc­trine is that of an all-pervading u­ni­ty.ʼ The dis­ci­ple Tsang replied, ʼYes.ʼ The Mas­ter went out, and the oth­er dis­ci­ples asked, say­ing, ʼWhat do his words mean?ʼ Tsang said, ʼThe doc­trine of our mas­ter is to be true to the prin­ci­ples of our na­ture and the be­nev­o­lent ex­er­cise of them to oth­ers,-- this and noth­ing more.ʼ

4:16 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe mind of the su­pe­ri­or man is con­ver­sant with right­eous­ness; the mind of the mean man is con­ver­sant with gain.ʼ

4:17 The Mas­ter said, ʼWhen we see men of worth, we should think of e­qual­ling them; when we see men of a con­tra­ry char­ac­ter, we should turn in­wards and ex­am­ine our­selves.ʼ

4:18 The Mas­ter said, ʼIn serv­ing his par­ents, a son may re­mon­strate with them, but gen­tly; when he sees that they do not in­cline to fol­low his ad­vice, he shows an in­creased de­gree of rev­er­ence, but does not a­ban­don his pur­pose; and should they pun­ish him, he does not al­low him­self to mur­mur.ʼ

4:19 The Mas­ter said, ʼWhile his par­ents are a­live, the son may not go a­broad to a dis­tance. If he does go a­broad, he must have a fixed place to which he goes.ʼ

4:20 The Mas­ter said, ʼIf the son for three years does not al­ter from the way of his fa­ther, he may be called fil­ial.ʼ

4:21 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe years of par­ents may by no means not be kept in the mem­o­ry, as an oc­ca­sion at once for joy and for fear.ʼ

4:22 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe rea­son why the an­cients did not read­i­ly give ut­ter­ance to their words, was that they feared lest their ac­tions should not come up to them.ʼ

4:23 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe cau­tious sel­dom err.ʼ

4:24 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man wishes to be slow in his speech and ear­nest in his con­duct.ʼ

4:25 The Mas­ter said, ʼVirtue is not left to stand a­lone. He who prac­tises it will have neigh­bors.ʼ

4:26 Tsze-yu said, ʼIn serv­ing a prince, fre­quent re­mon­strances lead to dis­grace. Be­tween friends, fre­quent re­proofs make the friend­ship dis­tant.ʼ

Book 5

5:1 The Mas­ter said of Kung-ye Chʼang that he might be wived; al­though he was put in bonds, he had not been guilt­y of any crime. Ac­cord­ing­ly, he gave him his own daugh­ter to wife. Of Nan Yung he said that if the coun­try were well gov­erned he would not be out of of­fice, and if it were ill-governed, he would es­cape pun­ish­ment and dis­grace. He gave him the daugh­ter of his own eld­er broth­er to wife.

5:2 The Mas­ter said of Tsze-chien, ʼOf su­pe­ri­or vir­tue in­deed is such a man! If there were not vir­tu­ous men in Lu, how could this man have ac­quired this char­ac­ter?ʼ

5:3 Tsze-kung asked, ʼWhat do you say of me, Tsʼze? The Mas­ter said, ʼYou are a u­ten­sil.ʼ ʼWhat u­ten­sil?ʼ ʼA gemmed sac­ri­fi­cial u­ten­sil.ʼ

5:4 Some one said, ʼYung is tru­ly vir­tu­ous, but he is not read­y with his tongue.ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼWhat is the good of be­ing read­y with the tongue? They who en­coun­ter men with smartnesses of speech for the most part pro­cure them­selves ha­tred. I know not wheth­er he be tru­ly vir­tu­ous, but why should he show read­i­ness of the tongue?ʼ

5:5 The Mas­ter was wishing Chʼi-tiao Kʼai to en­ter on of­fi­cial em­ploy­ment. He replied, ʼI am not yet a­ble to rest in the as­sur­ance of THIS.ʼ The Mas­ter was pleased.

5:6 The Mas­ter said, ʼMy doc­trines make no way. I will get up­on a raft, and float a­bout on the sea. He that will ac­com­pa­ny me will be Yu, I dare say.ʼ Tsze-lu hear­ing this was glad, up­on which the Mas­ter said, ʼYu is fonder of dar­ing than I am. He does not ex­er­cise his judg­ment up­on mat­ters.ʼ

5:7 Mang Wu asked a­bout Tsze-lu, wheth­er he was per­fect­ly vir­tu­ous. The Mas­ter said, ʼI do not know.ʼ He asked a­gain, when the Mas­ter replied, ʼIn a king­dom of a thou­sand char­i­ots, Yu might be em­ployed to man­age the mil­i­tar­y lev­ies, but I do not know wheth­er he be per­fect­ly vir­tu­ous.ʼ ʼAnd what do you say of Chʼiu?ʼ The Mas­ter replied, ʼIn a cit­y of a thou­sand families, or a clan of a hun­dred char­i­ots, Chʼiu might be em­ployed as gov­er­nor, but I do not know wheth­er he is per­fect­ly vir­tu­ous.ʼ ʼWhat do you say of Chʼih?ʼ The Mas­ter replied, ʼWith his sash girt and stand­ing in a court, Chʼih might be em­ployed to con­verse with the vis­i­tors and guests, but I do not know wheth­er he is per­fect­ly vir­tu­ous.ʼ

5:8 The Mas­ter said to Tsze-kung, ʼWhich do you con­sid­er su­pe­ri­or, your­self or Hui?ʼ Tsze-kung replied, ʼHow dare I com­pare my­self with Hui? Hui hears one point and knows all a­bout a sub­ject; I hear one point, and know a sec­ond.ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼYou are not e­qual to him. I grant you, you are not e­qual to him.ʼ

5:9 Tsai Yu be­ing a­sleep dur­ing the day­time, the Mas­ter said, ʼRotten wood can­not be carved; a wall of dirt­y earth will not re­ceive the trow­el. This Yu!-- what is the use of my re­prov­ing him?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼAt first, my way with men was to hear their words, and give them cred­it for their con­duct. Now my way is to hear their words, and look at their con­duct. It is from Yu that I have learn­ed to make this change.ʼ

5:10 The Mas­ter said, ʼI have not seen a firm and un­bend­ing man.ʼ Some one replied, ʼThere is Shan Chʼang.ʼ ʼChʼang,ʼ said the Mas­ter, ʼis un­der the in­flu­ence of his pas­sions; how can he be pro­nounced firm and un­bend­ing?ʼ

5:11 Tsze-kung said, ʼWhat I do not wish men to do to me, I al­so wish not to do to men.ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼTsʼze, you have not at­tained to that.ʼ

5:12 Tsze-kung said, ʼThe Mas­terʼs per­son­al dis­plays of his prin­ci­ples and or­di­nar­y de­scrip­tions of them may be heard. His dis­courses a­bout manʼs na­ture, and the way of Heav­en, can­not be heard.ʼ

5:13 When Tsze-lu heard any­thing, if he had not yet suc­ceeded in car­ry­ing it in­to prac­tice, he was on­ly a­fraid lest he should hear some­thing else.

5:14 Tsze-kung asked, say­ing, ʼOn what ground did Kung-wan get that ti­tle of Wan?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼHe was of an ac­tive na­ture and yet fond of learn­ing, and he was not a­shamed to ask and learn of his in­fe­ri­ors!-- On these grounds he has been styled Wan.ʼ

5:15 The Mas­ter said of Tsze-chʼan that he had four of the char­ac­ter­is­tics of a su­pe­ri­or man:-- in his con­duct of him­self, he was hum­ble; in serv­ing his su­pe­ri­ors, he was re­spect­ful; in nourishing the peo­ple, he was kind; in ordering the peo­ple, he was just.ʼ

5:16 The Mas­ter said, ʼYen Pʼing knew well how to main­tain friend­ly in­ter­course. The ac­quaint­ance might be long, but he showed the same re­spect as at first.ʼ

5:17 The Mas­ter said, ʼTsang Wan kept a large tor­toise in a house, on the cap­i­tals of the pil­lars of which he had hills made, and with rep­re­sen­ta­tions of duck­weed on the small pil­lars a­bove the beams sup­port­ing the raft­ers.-- Of what sort was his wis­dom?ʼ

5:18 Tsze-chang asked, say­ing, ʼThe min­is­ter Tsze- wan thrice took of­fice, and man­i­fested no joy in his coun­te­nance. Thrice he re­tired from of­fice, and man­i­fested no dis­pleas­ure. He made it a point to in­form the new min­is­ter of the way in which he had con­ducted the gov­ern­ment;-- what do you say of him?ʼ The Mas­ter replied. ʼHe was loy­al.ʼ ʼWas he per­fect­ly vir­tu­ous?ʼ ʼI do not know. How can he be pro­nounced per­fect­ly vir­tu­ous?ʼ Tsze-chang pro­ceeded, ʼWhen the of­fic­er Chʼui killed the prince of Chʼi, Chʼan Wan, though he was the own­er of for­ty hors­es, a­ban­doned them and left the coun­try. Com­ing to an­oth­er State, he said, "They are here like our great of­fic­er, Chʼui," and left it. He came to a sec­ond State, and with the same ob­ser­va­tion left it al­so;-- what do you say of him?ʼ The Mas­ter replied, ʼHe was pure.ʼ ʼWas he per­fect­ly vir­tu­ous?ʼ ʼI do not know. How can he be pro­nounced per­fect­ly vir­tu­ous?ʼ

5:19 Chi Wan thought thrice, and then acted. When the Mas­ter was in­formed of it, he said, ʼTwice may do.ʼ

5:20 The Mas­ter said, ʼWhen good or­der pre­vailed in his coun­try, Ning Wu acted the part of a wise man. When his coun­try was in dis­or­der, he acted the part of a stu­pid man. Oth­ers may e­qual his wis­dom, but they can­not e­qual his stu­pid­i­ty.ʼ

5:21 When the Mas­ter was in Chʼan, he said, ʼLet me re­turn! Let me re­turn! The lit­tle chil­dren of my school are am­bi­tious and too hast­y. They are ac­com­plished and com­plete so far, but they do not know how to re­strict and shape them­selves.ʼ

5:22 The Mas­ter said, ʼPo-i and Shu-chʼi did not keep the form­er wickednesses of men in mind, and hence the re­sent­ments di­rect­ed to­wards them were few.ʼ

5:23 The Mas­ter said, ʼWho says of Wei-shang Kao that he is up­right? One begged some vin­e­gar of him, and he begged it of a neigh­bor and gave it to the man.ʼ

5:24 The Mas­ter said, ʼFine words, an in­sin­u­at­ing ap­pear­ance, and ex­ces­sive re­spect;-- Tso Chʼiu-ming was a­shamed of them. I al­so am a­shamed of them. To con­ceal re­sent­ment a­gainst a per­son, and ap­pear friend­ly with him;-- Tso Chʼiu-ming was a­shamed of such con­duct. I al­so am a­shamed of it.ʼ

5:25 Yen Yu­an and Chi Lu be­ing by his side, the Mas­ter said to them, ʼCome, let each of you tell his wishes.ʼ Tsze-lu said, ʼI should like, hav­ing char­i­ots and hors­es, and light fur dresses, to share them with my friends, and though they should spoil them, I would not be dis­pleased.ʼ Yen Yu­an said, ʼI should like not to boast of my ex­cel­lence, nor to make a dis­play of my mer­i­to­ri­ous deeds.ʼ Tsze-lu then said, ʼI should like, sir, to hear your wishes.ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼThey are, in re­gard to the a­ged, to give them rest; in re­gard to friends, to show them sin­cer­i­ty; in re­gard to the young, to treat them ten­der­ly.ʼ

5:26 The Mas­ter said, ʼIt is all o­ver! I have not yet seen one who could per­ceive his faults, and in­ward­ly ac­cuse him­self.ʼ

5:27 The Mas­ter said, ʼIn a ham­let of ten families, there may be found one honourable and sin­cere as I am, but not so fond of learn­ing.ʼ

Book 6

6:1 The Mas­ter said, ʼThere is Yung!-- He might oc­cu­py the place of a prince.ʼ Chung-kung asked a­bout Tsze-sang Po-tsze. The Mas­ter said, ʼHe may pass. He does not mind small mat­ters.ʼ Chung-kung said, ʼIf a man cher­ish in him­self a rev­er­en­tial feel­ing of the ne­ces­si­ty of at­ten­tion to busi­ness, though he may be eas­y in small mat­ters in his gov­ern­ment of the peo­ple, that may be al­lowed. But if he cher­ish in him­self that eas­y feel­ing, and al­so car­ry it out in his prac­tice, is not such an eas­y mode of pro­ce­dure ex­ces­sive?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼYungʼs words are right.ʼ

6:2 The Duke A­i asked which of the dis­ci­ples loved to learn. Confucius replied to him, ʼThere was Yen Hui; HE loved to learn. He did not trans­fer his an­ger; he did not re­peat a fault. Un­for­tu­nate­ly, his ap­point­ed time was short and he died; and now there is not such an­oth­er. I have not yet heard of any one who loves to learn as he did.ʼ

6:3 Tsze-hwa be­ing em­ployed on a mis­sion to Chʼi, the dis­ci­ple Zan re­quested grain for his moth­er. The Mas­ter said, ʼGive her a fu.ʼ Yen re­quested more. ʼGive her an yu,ʼ said the Mas­ter. Yen gave her five ping. The Mas­ter said, ʼWhen Chʼih was pro­ceed­ing to Chʼi, he had fat hors­es to his car­riage, and wore light furs. I have heard that a su­pe­ri­or man helps the dis­tressed, but does not add to the wealth of the rich.ʼ Yu­an Sze be­ing made gov­er­nor of his town by the Mas­ter, he gave him nine hun­dred meas­ures of grain, but Sze de­clined them. The Mas­ter said, ʼDo not de­cline them. May you not give them a­way in the neigh­bor­hoods, ham­lets, towns, and vil­lages?ʼ

6:4 The Mas­ter, speak­ing of Chung-kung, said, ʼIf the calf of a brin­dled cow be red and horned, al­though men may not wish to use it, would the spir­its of the moun­tains and riv­ers put it a­side?ʼ

6:5 The Mas­ter said, ʼSuch was Hui that for three months there would be noth­ing in his mind con­tra­ry to per­fect vir­tue. The oth­ers may at­tain to this on some days or in some months, but noth­ing more.ʼ

6:6 Chi Kʼang asked a­bout Chung-yu, wheth­er he was fit to be em­ployed as an of­fic­er of gov­ern­ment. The Mas­ter said, ʼYu is a man of de­ci­sion; what dif­fi­cul­ty would he find in be­ing an of­fic­er of gov­ern­ment?ʼ Kʼang asked, ʼIs Tsʼze fit to be em­ployed as an of­fic­er of gov­ern­ment?ʼ and was an­swered, ʼTsʼze is a man of in­tel­li­gence; what dif­fi­cul­ty would he find in be­ing an of­fic­er of gov­ern­ment?ʼ And to the same ques­tion a­bout Chʼiu the Mas­ter gave the same re­ply, say­ing, ʼChʼiu is a man of var­i­ous a­bil­i­ty.ʼ

6:7 The chief of the Chi fam­i­ly sent to ask Min Tsze-chʼien to be gov­er­nor of Pi. Min Tsze-chʼien said, ʼDecline the of­fer for me po­lite­ly. If any one come a­gain to me with a sec­ond in­vi­ta­tion, I shall be o­bliged to go and live on the banks of the Wan.ʼ

6:8 Po-niu be­ing ill, the Mas­ter went to ask for him. He took hold of his hand through the win­dow, and said, ʼIt is kill­ing him. It is the ap­point­ment of Heav­en, a­las! That such a man should have such a sick­ness! That such a man should have such a sick­ness!ʼ

6:9 The Mas­ter said, ʼAdmirable in­deed was the vir­tue of Hui! With a sin­gle bam­boo dish of rice, a sin­gle gourd dish of drink, and liv­ing in his mean nar­row lane, while oth­ers could not have en­dured the dis­tress, he did not al­low his joy to be af­fect­ed by it. Ad­mi­ra­ble in­deed was the vir­tue of Hui!ʼ

6:10 Yen Chʼiu said, ʼIt is not that I do not de­light in your doc­trines, but my strength is in­suf­fi­cient.ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼThose whose strength is in­suf­fi­cient give o­ver in the mid­dle of the way but now you lim­it your­self.ʼ

6:11 The Mas­ter said to Tsze-hsia, ʼDo you be a schol­ar af­ter the style of the su­pe­ri­or man, and not af­ter that of the mean man.ʼ

6:12 Tsze-yu be­ing gov­er­nor of Wu-chʼang, the Mas­ter said to him, ʼHave you got good men there?ʼ He an­swered, ʼThere is Tan-tʼai Mieh-ming, who nev­er in walk­ing takes a short cut, and nev­er co­mes to my of­fice, ex­cept­ing on pub­lic busi­ness.ʼ

6:13 The Mas­ter said, ʼMang Chih-fan does not boast of his mer­it. Be­ing in the rear on an oc­ca­sion of flight, when they were a­bout to en­ter the gate, he whipped up his horse, say­ing, "It is not that I dare to be last. My horse would not ad­vance."ʼ

6:14 The Mas­ter said, ʼWithout the spe­cious speech of the litanist Tʼo and the beau­ty of the prince Chao of Sung, it is dif­fi­cult to es­cape in the pres­ent age.ʼ

6:15 The Mas­ter said, ʼWho can go out but by the door? How is it that men will not walk ac­cord­ing to these ways?ʼ

6:16 The Mas­ter said, ʼWhere the sol­id qualities are in ex­cess of ac­com­plish­ments, we have rusticity; where the ac­com­plish­ments are in ex­cess of the sol­id qualities, we have the man­ners of a clerk. When the ac­com­plish­ments and sol­id qualities are e­qual­ly blend­ed, we then have the man of vir­tue.ʼ

6:17 The Mas­ter said, ʼMan is born for uprightness. If a man lose his uprightness, and yet live, his es­cape from death is the ef­fect of mer­e good for­tune.ʼ

6:18 The Mas­ter said, ʼThey who know the truth are not e­qual to those who love it, and they who love it are not e­qual to those who de­light in it.ʼ

6:19 The Mas­ter said, ʼTo those whose tal­ents are a­bove me­di­oc­ri­ty, the highest sub­jects may be an­nounced. To those who are be­low me­di­oc­ri­ty, the highest sub­jects may not be an­nounced.ʼ

6:20 Fan Chʼih asked what con­sti­tut­ed wis­dom. The Mas­ter said, ʼTo give oneʼs self ear­nest­ly to the duties due to men, and, while re­spect­ing spir­it­u­al be­ings, to keep a­loof from them, may be called wis­dom.ʼ He asked a­bout per­fect vir­tue. The Mas­ter said, ʼThe man of vir­tue makes the dif­fi­cul­ty to be o­ver­come his first busi­ness, and suc­cess on­ly a sub­se­quent con­sid­er­a­tion;-- this may be called per­fect vir­tue.ʼ

6:21 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe wise find pleas­ure in wa­ter; the vir­tu­ous find pleas­ure in hills. The wise are ac­tive; the vir­tu­ous are tran­quil. The wise are joy­ful; the vir­tu­ous are long-lived.ʼ

6:22 The Mas­ter said, ʼChʼi, by one change, would come to the State of Lu. Lu, by one change, would come to a State where true prin­ci­ples pre­dom­i­nat­ed.ʼ

6:23 The Mas­ter said, ʼA cor­nered ves­sel with­out cor­ners.-- A strange cor­nered ves­sel! A strange cor­nered ves­sel!ʼ

6:24 Tsai Wo asked, say­ing, ʼA be­nev­o­lent man, though it be told him,-- ʼThere is a man in the wellʼ will go in af­ter him, I sup­pose.ʼ Confucius said, ʼWhy should he do so?ʼ A su­pe­ri­or man may be made to go to the well, but he can­not be made to go down in­to it. He may be im­posed up­on, but he can­not be fooled.ʼ

6:25 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man, ex­ten­sive­ly stud­y­ing all learn­ing, and keep­ing him­self un­der the re­straint of the rules of pro­pri­e­ty, may thus like­wise not o­ver­step what is right.ʼ

6:26 The Mas­ter hav­ing vis­ited Nan-tsze, Tsze-lu was dis­pleased, on which the Mas­ter swore, say­ing, ʼWherein I have done im­prop­er­ly, may Heav­en re­ject me, may Heav­en re­ject me!ʼ

6:27 The Mas­ter said, ʼPerfect is the vir­tue which is ac­cord­ing to the Con­stant Mean! Rare for a long time has been its prac­tise a­mong the peo­ple.ʼ

6:28 Tsze-kung said, ʼSuppose the case of a man ex­ten­sive­ly con­fer­ring ben­e­fits on the peo­ple, and a­ble to as­sist all, what would you say of him? Might he be called per­fect­ly vir­tu­ous?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼWhy speak on­ly of vir­tue in con­nex­ion with him? Must he not have the qualities of a sage? E­ven Yao and Shun were still so­lic­i­tous a­bout this. ʼNow the man of per­fect vir­tue, wishing to be es­tab­lished him­self, seeks al­so to es­tab­lish oth­ers; wishing to be en­larged him­self, he seeks al­so to en­large oth­ers. ʼTo be a­ble to judge of oth­ers by what is nigh in our­selves;-- this may be called the art of vir­tue.ʼ

Book 7

7:1 The Mas­ter said, ʼA trans­mit­ter and not a mak­er, believing in and lov­ing the an­cients, I ven­ture to com­pare my­self with our old Pʼang.ʼ

7:2 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe si­lent treas­ur­ing up of knowl­edge; learn­ing with­out sa­ti­e­ty; and instructing oth­ers with­out be­ing wea­ried:-- which one of these things be­longs to me?ʼ

7:3 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe leav­ing vir­tue with­out prop­er cul­ti­va­tion; the not thor­ough­ly discussing what is learn­ed; not be­ing a­ble to move to­wards right­eous­ness of which a knowl­edge is gained; and not be­ing a­ble to change what is not good:-- these are the things which oc­ca­sion me so­lic­i­tude.ʼ

7:4 When the Mas­ter was un­oc­cu­pied with busi­ness, his man­ner was eas­y, and he looked pleased.

7:5 The Mas­ter said, ʼExtreme is my de­cay. For a long time, I have not dreamed, as I was wont to do, that I saw the duke of Chau.ʼ

7:6 The Mas­ter said, ʼLet the will be set on the path of du­ty. ʼLet eve­ry at­tain­ment in what is good be firm­ly grasped. ʼLet per­fect vir­tue be ac­corded with. ʼLet re­lax­a­tion and en­joy­ment be found in the po­lite arts.ʼ

7:7 The Mas­ter said, ʼFrom the man bring­ing his bun­dle of dried flesh for my teach­ing up­wards, I have nev­er re­fused in­struc­tion to any one.ʼ

7:8 The Mas­ter said, ʼI do not o­pen up the truth to one who is not ea­ger to get knowl­edge, nor help out any one who is not anx­ious to ex­plain him­self. When I have pre­sented one cor­ner of a sub­ject to any one, and he can­not from it learn the oth­er three, I do not re­peat my les­son.ʼ

7:9 When the Mas­ter was eat­ing by the side of a mourn­er, he nev­er ate to the full. He did not sing on the same day in which he had been weep­ing.

7:10 The Mas­ter said to Yen Yu­an, ʼWhen called to of­fice, to un­der­take its duties; when not so called, to lie re­tired;-- it is on­ly I and you who have at­tained to this.ʼ Tsze-lu said, ʼIf you had the con­duct of the armies of a great State, whom would you have to act with you?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼI would not have him to act with me, who will un­armed at­tack a ti­ger, or cross a riv­er with­out a boat, dy­ing with­out any re­gret. My as­so­ci­ate must be the man who pro­ceeds to ac­tion full of so­lic­i­tude, who is fond of adjusting his plans, and then carries them in­to ex­e­cu­tion.ʼ

7:11 The Mas­ter said, ʼIf the search for rich­es is sure to be suc­cess­ful, though I should be­come a groom with whip in hand to get them, I will do so. As the search may not be suc­cess­ful, I will fol­low af­ter that which I love.ʼ

7:12 The things in ref­er­ence to which the Mas­ter ex­er­cised the great­est cau­tion were -- fasting, war, and sick­ness.

7:13 When the Mas­ter was in Chʼi, he heard the Shao, and for three months did not know the taste of flesh. ʼI did not thinkʼʼ he said, ʼthat mu­sic could have been made so ex­cel­lent as this.ʼ

7:14 Yen Yu said, ʼIs our Mas­ter for the rul­er of Wei?ʼ Tsze-kung said, ʼOh! I will ask him.ʼ He went in ac­cord­ing­ly, and said, ʼWhat sort of men were Po-i and Shu-chʼi?ʼ ʼThey were an­cient worthies,ʼ said the Mas­ter. ʼDid they have any re­pin­ings be­cause of their course?ʼ The Mas­ter a­gain replied, ʼThey sought to act vir­tu­ous­ly, and they did so; what was there for them to re­pine a­bout?ʼ On this, Tsze-kung went out and said, ʼOur Mas­ter is not for him.ʼ

7:15 The Mas­ter said, ʼWith coarse rice to eat, with wa­ter to drink, and my bend­ed arm for a pil­low;-- I have still joy in the midst of these things. Rich­es and honours ac­quired by unrighteousness, are to me as a float­ing cloud.ʼ

7:16 The Mas­ter said, ʼIf some years were added to my life, I would give fif­ty to the stud­y of the Yi, and then I might come to be with­out great faults.ʼ

7:17 The Mas­terʼs fre­quent themes of dis­course were-- the Odes, the His­to­ry, and the main­te­nance of the Rules of Pro­pri­e­ty. On all these he fre­quent­ly dis­coursed.

7:18 The Duke of Sheh asked Tsze-lu a­bout Confucius, and Tsze-lu did not an­swer him. The Mas­ter said, ʼWhy did you not say to him,-- He is simp­ly a man, who in his ea­ger pur­suit (of knowl­edge) for­gets his food, who in the joy of its at­tain­ment for­gets his sor­rows, and who does not per­ceive that old age is com­ing on?ʼ

7:19 The Mas­ter said, ʼI am not one who was born in the pos­ses­sion of knowl­edge; I am one who is fond of an­tiq­ui­ty, and ear­nest in seek­ing it there.ʼ

7:20 The sub­jects on which the Mas­ter did not talk, were-- ex­traor­di­nar­y things, feats of strength, dis­or­der, and spir­it­u­al be­ings.

7:21 The Mas­ter said, ʼWhen I walk a­long with two oth­ers, they may serve me as my teach­ers. I will se­lect their good qualities and fol­low them, their bad qualities and a­void them.ʼ

7:22 The Mas­ter said, ʼHeaven pro­duced the vir­tue that is in me. Hwan Tʼui-- what can he do to me?ʼ

7:23 The Mas­ter said, ʼDo you think, my dis­ci­ples, that I have any con­ceal­ments? I con­ceal noth­ing from you. There is noth­ing which I do that is not shown to you, my dis­ci­ples;-- that is my way.ʼ

7:24 There were four things which the Mas­ter taught,-- let­ters, eth­ics, de­vo­tion of soul, and truthfulness.

7:25 The Mas­ter said, ʼA sage it is not mine to see; could I see a man of re­al tal­ent and vir­tue, that would sat­is­fy me.ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼA good man it is not mine to see; could I see a man pos­sessed of con­stan­cy, that would sat­is­fy me. ʼHaving not and yet af­fect­ing to have, emp­ty and yet af­fect­ing to be full, strait­ened and yet af­fect­ing to be at ease:-- it is dif­fi­cult with such char­ac­ter­is­tics to have con­stan­cy.ʼ

7:26 The Mas­ter an­gled,-- but did not use a net. He shot,-- but not at birds perching.

7:27 The Mas­ter said, ʼThere may be those who act with­out know­ing why. I do not do so. Hear­ing much and selecting what is good and fol­low­ing it; see­ing much and keep­ing it in mem­o­ry:-- this is the sec­ond style of knowl­edge.ʼ

7:28 It was dif­fi­cult to talk (profitably and rep­u­ta­bly) with the peo­ple of Hu-hsiang, and a lad of that place hav­ing had an in­ter­view with the Mas­ter, the dis­ci­ples doubted. The Mas­ter said, ʼI ad­mit peo­pleʼs ap­proach to me with­out com­mit­ting my­self as to what they may do when they have re­tired. Why must one be so se­vere? If a man pu­ri­fy him­self to wait up­on me, I re­ceive him so pu­ri­fied, with­out guar­an­tee­ing his past con­duct.ʼ

7:29 The Mas­ter said, ʼIs vir­tue a thing re­mote? I wish to be vir­tu­ous, and lo! vir­tue is at hand.ʼ

7:30 The min­is­ter of crime of Chʼan asked wheth­er the duke Chao knew pro­pri­e­ty, and Confucius said, ʼHe knew pro­pri­e­ty.ʼ Confucius hav­ing re­tired, the min­is­ter bowed to Wu-ma Chʼi to come for­ward, and said, ʼI have heard that the su­pe­ri­or man is not a par­ti­san. May the su­pe­ri­or man be a par­ti­san al­so? The prince mar­ried a daugh­ter of the house of Wu, of the same sur­name with him­self, and called her,-- "The eld­er Tsze of Wu." If the prince knew pro­pri­e­ty, who does not know it?ʼ Wu-ma Chʼi re­ported these re­marks, and the Mas­ter said, ʼI am for­tu­nate! If I have any er­rors, peo­ple are sure to know them.ʼ

7:31 When the Mas­ter was in com­pa­ny with a per­son who was sing­ing, if he sang well, he would make him re­peat the song, while he ac­com­pa­nied it with his own voice.

7:32 The Mas­ter said, ʼIn let­ters I am per­haps e­qual to oth­er men, but the char­ac­ter of the su­pe­ri­or man, car­ry­ing out in his con­duct what he professes, is what I have not yet at­tained to.ʼ

7:33 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe sage and the man of per­fect vir­tue;-- how dare I rank my­self with them? It may simp­ly be said of me, that I strive to be­come such with­out sa­ti­e­ty, and teach oth­ers with­out wea­ri­ness.ʼ Kung-hsi Hwa said, ʼThis is just what we, the dis­ci­ples, can­not im­i­tate you in.ʼ

7:34 The Mas­ter be­ing ver­y sick, Tsze-lu asked leave to pray for him. He said, ʼMay such a thing be done?ʼ Tsze-lu replied, ʼIt may. In the Eulogies it is said, "Prayer has been made for thee to the spir­its of the up­per and low­er worlds."ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼMy praying has been for a long time.ʼ

7:35 The Mas­ter said, ʼExtravagance leads to in­sub­or­di­na­tion, and par­si­mo­ny to meanness. It is bet­ter to be mean than to be in­sub­or­di­nate.ʼ

7:36 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man is sat­is­fied and com­posed; the mean man is al­ways full of dis­tress.ʼ

7:37 The Mas­ter was mild, and yet dig­ni­fied; ma­jes­tic, and yet not fierce; re­spect­ful, and yet eas­y.

Book 8

8:1 The Mas­ter said, ʼTʼai-po may be said to have reached the highest point of vir­tu­ous ac­tion. Thrice he de­clined the king­dom, and the peo­ple in ig­no­rance of his mo­tives could not ex­press their ap­pro­ba­tion of his con­duct.ʼ

8:2 The Mas­ter said, ʼRespectfulness, with­out the rules of pro­pri­e­ty, be­comes la­bo­ri­ous bus­tle; care­ful­ness, with­out the rules of pro­pri­e­ty, be­comes ti­mid­i­ty; bold­ness, with­out the rules of pro­pri­e­ty, be­comes in­sub­or­di­na­tion; straight­for­ward­ness, with­out the rules of pro­pri­e­ty, be­comes rude­ness. ʼWhen those who are in high sta­tions per­form well all their duties to their re­la­tions, the peo­ple are a­roused to vir­tue. When old friends are not ne­glected by them, the peo­ple are pre­served from meanness.ʼ

8:3 The phi­los­o­pher Tsang be­ing ill, he called to him the dis­ci­ples of his school, and said, ʼUncover my feet, un­cover my hands. It is said in the Book of Po­et­ry, "We should be ap­pre­hen­sive and cau­tious, as if on the brink of a deep gulf, as if tread­ing on thin ice," and so have I been. Now and here­af­ter, I know my es­cape from all in­ju­ry to my per­son, O ye, my lit­tle chil­dren.ʼ

8:4 1. The phi­los­o­pher Tsang be­ing ill, Meng Chang went to ask how he was. Tsang said to him, ʼWhen a bird is a­bout to die, its notes are mourn­ful; when a man is a­bout to die, his words are good. ʼThere are three prin­ci­ples of con­duct which the man of high rank should con­sid­er spe­cial­ly im­por­tant:-- that in his de­port­ment and man­ner he keep from vi­o­lence and heed­less­ness; that in reg­u­lat­ing his coun­te­nance he keep near to sin­cer­i­ty; and that in his words and tones he keep far from low­ness and im­pro­pri­e­ty. As to such mat­ters as at­tend­ing to the sac­ri­fi­cial ves­sels, there are the prop­er of­fic­ers for them.ʼ

8:5 The phi­los­o­pher Tsang said, ʼGifted with a­bil­i­ty, and yet put­ting ques­tions to those who were not so; pos­sessed of much, and yet put­ting ques­tions to those pos­sessed of lit­tle; hav­ing, as though he had not; full, and yet counting him­self as emp­ty; of­fended a­gainst, and yet entering in­to no al­ter­ca­tion; for­mer­ly I had a friend who pur­sued this style of con­duct.ʼ

8:6 The phi­los­o­pher Tsang said, ʼSuppose that there is an in­di­vid­u­al who can be en­trusted with the charge of a young or­phan prince, and can be com­mis­sioned with au­thor­i­ty o­ver a state of a hun­dred li, and whom no e­mer­gen­cy how­ev­er great can drive from his prin­ci­ples:-- is such a man a su­pe­ri­or man? He is a su­pe­ri­or man in­deed.ʼ

8:7 1. The phi­los­o­pher Tsang said, ʼThe of­fic­er may not be with­out breadth of mind and vig­or­ous en­dur­ance. His bur­den is heav­y and his course is long. ʼPerfect vir­tue is the bur­den which he con­sid­ers it is his to sus­tain;-- is it not heav­y? On­ly with death does his course stop;-- is it not long?

8:8 The Mas­ter said, ʼIt is by the Odes that the mind is a­roused. ʼIt is by the Rules of Pro­pri­e­ty that the char­ac­ter is es­tab­lished. ʼIt is from Mu­sic that the fin­ish is re­ceived.ʼ

8:9 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe peo­ple may be made to fol­low a path of ac­tion, but they may not be made to un­der­stand it.ʼ

8:10 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe man who is fond of dar­ing and is dis­sat­is­fied with pov­er­ty, will pro­ceed to in­sub­or­di­na­tion. So will the man who is not vir­tu­ous, when you car­ry your dis­like of him to an ex­treme.ʼ

8:11 The Mas­ter said, ʼThough a man have abilities as ad­mi­ra­ble as those of the Duke of Chau, yet if he be proud and nig­gard­ly, those oth­er things are re­al­ly not worth be­ing looked at.ʼ

8:12 The Mas­ter said, ʼIt is not eas­y to find a man who has learn­ed for three years with­out com­ing to be good.ʼ

8:13 The Mas­ter said, ʼWith sin­cere faith he u­nites the love of learn­ing; hold­ing firm to death, he is perfecting the ex­cel­lence of his course. ʼSuch an one will not en­ter a tot­ter­ing State, nor dwell in a dis­or­gan­ized one. When right prin­ci­ples of gov­ern­ment pre­vail in the king­dom, he will show him­self; when they are prostrated, he will keep con­cealed. ʼWhen a coun­try is well-governed, pov­er­ty and a mean con­di­tion are things to be a­shamed of. When a coun­try is ill- gov­erned, rich­es and honour are things to be a­shamed of.ʼ

8:14 The Mas­ter said, ʼHe who is not in any par­tic­u­lar of­fice, has noth­ing to do with plans for the ad­min­is­tra­tion of its duties.ʼ

8:15 The Mas­ter said, ʼWhen the mu­sic mas­ter Chih first en­tered on his of­fice, the fin­ish of the Kwan Tsu was mag­nif­i­cent;-- how it filled the ears!ʼ

8:16 The Mas­ter said, ʼArdent and yet not up­right; stu­pid and yet not at­ten­tive; sim­ple and yet not sin­cere:-- such per­sons I do not un­der­stand.ʼ

8:17 The Mas­ter said, ʼLearn as if you could not reach your ob­ject, and were al­ways fearing al­so lest you should lose it.ʼ

8:18 The Mas­ter said, ʼHow ma­jes­tic was the man­ner in which Shun and Yu held pos­ses­sion of the em­pire, as if it were noth­ing to them!ʼ

8:19 The Mas­ter said, ʼGreat in­deed was Yao as a sov­er­eign! How ma­jes­tic was he! It is on­ly Heav­en that is grand, and on­ly Yao cor­re­sponded to it. How vast was his vir­tue! The peo­ple could find no name for it. ʼHow ma­jes­tic was he in the works which he ac­com­plished! How glo­ri­ous in the el­e­gant reg­u­la­tions which he in­sti­tut­ed!ʼ

8:20 Shun had five min­is­ters, and the em­pire was well-governed. King Wu said, ʼI have ten a­ble min­is­ters.ʼ Confucius said, ʼIs not the say­ing that tal­ents are dif­fi­cult to find, true? On­ly when the dynasties of Tʼang and Yu met, were they more a­bun­dant than in this of Chau, yet there was a wom­an a­mong them. The a­ble min­is­ters were no more than nine men. ʼKing Wan pos­sessed two of the three parts of the em­pire, and with those he served the dyn­as­ty of Yin. The vir­tue of the house of Chau may be said to have reached the highest point in­deed.ʼ

8:21 The Mas­ter said, ʼI can find no flaw in the char­ac­ter of Yu. He used him­self coarse food and drink, but dis­played the ut­most fil­ial pi­e­ty to­wards the spir­its. His or­di­nar­y gar­ments were poor, but he dis­played the ut­most el­e­gance in his sac­ri­fi­cial cap and a­pron. He lived in a low mean house, but ex­pended all his strength on the ditches and wa­ter-channels. I can find noth­ing like a flaw in Yu.ʼ

Book 9

9:1 The sub­jects of which the Mas­ter sel­dom spoke were-- prof­it­a­ble­ness, and al­so the ap­point­ments of Heav­en, and per­fect vir­tue.

9:2 A man of the vil­lage of Ta-hsiang said, ʼGreat in­deed is the phi­los­o­pher Kʼung! His learn­ing is ex­ten­sive, and yet he does not rend­er his name fa­mous by any par­tic­u­lar thing.ʼ The Mas­ter heard the ob­ser­va­tion, and said to his dis­ci­ples, ʼWhat shall I prac­tise? Shall I prac­tise charioteering, or shall I prac­tise ar­cher­y? I will prac­tise charioteering.ʼ

9:3 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe lin­en cap is that pre­scribed by the rules of cer­e­mo­ny, but now a silk one is worn. It is e­co­nom­i­cal, and I fol­low the com­mon prac­tice. ʼThe rules of cer­e­mo­ny pre­scribe the bow­ing be­low the hall, but now the prac­tice is to bow on­ly af­ter as­cend­ing it. That is ar­ro­gant. I con­tin­ue to bow be­low the hall, though I op­pose the com­mon prac­tice.ʼ

9:4 There were four things from which the Mas­ter was en­tire­ly free. He had no fore­gone con­clu­sions, no ar­bi­trar­y pre­de­ter­mi­na­tions, no ob­sti­na­cy, and no e­go­ism.

9:5 The Mas­ter was put in fear in Kʼwang. He said, ʼAfter the death of King Wan, was not the cause of truth lodged here in me? ʼIf Heav­en had wished to let this cause of truth per­ish, then I, a fu­ture mor­tal, should not have got such a re­la­tion to that cause. While Heav­en does not let the cause of truth per­ish, what can the peo­ple of Kʼwang do to me?ʼ

9:6 A high of­fic­er asked Tsze-kung, say­ing, ʼMay we not say that your Mas­ter is a sage? How var­i­ous is his a­bil­i­ty!ʼ Tsze-kung said, ʼCertainly Heav­en has en­dowed him un­lim­it­ed­ly. He is a­bout a sage. And, more­o­ver, his a­bil­i­ty is var­i­ous.ʼ The Mas­ter heard of the con­ver­sa­tion and said, ʼDoes the high of­fic­er know me? When I was young, my con­di­tion was low, and there­fore I ac­quired my a­bil­i­ty in man­y things, but they were mean mat­ters. Must the su­pe­ri­or man have such va­ri­e­ty of a­bil­i­ty? He does not need va­ri­e­ty of a­bil­i­ty.ʼ Lao said, ʼThe Mas­ter said, "Having no of­fi­cial em­ploy­ment, I ac­quired man­y arts."ʼ

9:7 The Mas­ter said, ʼAm I in­deed pos­sessed of knowl­edge? I am not know­ing. But if a mean per­son, who ap­pears quite emp­ty-like, ask any­thing of me, I set it forth from one end to the oth­er, and ex­haust it.ʼ

9:8 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe FANG bird does not come; the riv­er sends forth no map:-- it is all o­ver with me!ʼ

9:9 When the Mas­ter saw a per­son in a mourn­ing dress, or any one with the cap and up­per and low­er gar­ments of full dress, or a blind per­son, on ob­serv­ing them approaching, though they were young­er than him­self, he would rise up, and if he had to pass by them, he would do so hast­i­ly.

9:10 Yen Yu­an, in ad­mi­ra­tion of the Mas­terʼs doc­trines, sighed and said, ʼI looked up to them, and they seemed to be­come more high; I tried to pen­e­trate them, and they seemed to be­come more firm; I looked at them be­fore me, and sud­den­ly they seemed to be be­hind. ʼThe Mas­ter, by or­der­ly meth­od, skil­ful­ly leads men on. He en­larged my mind with learn­ing, and taught me the re­straints of pro­pri­e­ty. ʼWhen I wish to give o­ver the stud­y of his doc­trines, I can­not do so, and hav­ing ex­erted all my a­bil­i­ty, there seems some­thing to stand right up be­fore me; but though I wish to fol­low and lay hold of it, I re­al­ly find no way to do so.ʼ

9:11 The Mas­ter be­ing ver­y ill, Tsze-lu wished the dis­ci­ples to act as min­is­ters to him. Dur­ing a re­mis­sion of his ill­ness, he said, ʼLong has the con­duct of Yu been de­ceit­ful! By pretending to have min­is­ters when I have them not, whom should I im­pose up­on? Should I im­pose up­on Heav­en? ʼMoreover, than that I should die in the hands of min­is­ters, is it not bet­ter that I should die in the hands of you, my dis­ci­ples? And though I may not get a great bur­i­al, shall I die up­on the road?ʼ

9:12 Tsze-kung said, ʼThere is a beau­ti­ful gem here. Should I lay it up in a case and keep it? or should I seek for a good price and sell it?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼSell it! Sell it! But I would wait for one to of­fer the price.ʼ

9:13 The Mas­ter was wishing to go and live a­mong the nine wild tribes of the east. Some one said, ʼThey are rude. How can you do such a thing?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼIf a su­pe­ri­or man dwelt a­mong them, what rude­ness would there be?ʼ

9:14 The Mas­ter said, ʼI re­turned from Wei to Lu, and then the mu­sic was re­formed, and the pieces in the Roy­al songs and Praise songs all found their prop­er places.ʼ

9:15 The Mas­ter said, ʼAbroad, to serve the high min­is­ters and no­bles; at home, to serve oneʼs fa­ther and eld­er broth­ers; in all duties to the dead, not to dare not to ex­ert oneʼs self; and not to be o­ver­come of wine:-- which one of these things do I at­tain to?ʼ

9:16 The Mas­ter stand­ing by a stream, said, ʼIt passes on just like this, not ceas­ing day or night!ʼ

9:17 The Mas­ter said, ʼI have not seen one who loves vir­tue as he loves beau­ty.ʼ

9:18 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe pros­e­cu­tion of learn­ing may be com­pared to what may hap­pen in rais­ing a mound. If there want but one bas­ket of earth to com­plete the work, and I stop, the stop­ping is my own work. It may be com­pared to throwing down the earth on the lev­el ground. Though but one bas­ket­ful is thrown at a time, the ad­vanc­ing with it is my own go­ing for­ward.ʼ

9:19 The Mas­ter said, ʼNever flag­ging when I set forth any­thing to him;-- ah! that is Hui.ʼ

9:20 The Mas­ter said of Yen Yu­an, ʼAlas! I saw his con­stant ad­vance. I nev­er saw him stop in his prog­ress.ʼ

9:21 The Mas­ter said, ʼThere are cases in which the blade springs, but the plant does not go on to flow­er! There are cases where it flow­ers, but no fruit is sub­se­quent­ly pro­duced!ʼ

9:22 The Mas­ter said, ʼA youth is to be re­garded with re­spect. How do we know that his fu­ture will not be e­qual to our pres­ent? If he reach the age of for­ty or fif­ty, and has not made him­self heard of, then in­deed he will not be worth be­ing re­garded with re­spect.ʼ

9:25 The Mas­ter said, ʼCan men ref­use to as­sent to the words of strict ad­mo­ni­tion? But it is reforming the con­duct be­cause of them which is val­u­a­ble. Can men ref­use to be pleased with words of gen­tle ad­vice? But it is unfolding their aim which is val­u­a­ble. If a man be pleased with these words, but does not un­fold their aim, and as­sents to those, but does not re­form his con­duct, I can re­al­ly do noth­ing with him.ʼ

9:25 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe com­mand­er of the forces of a large state may be car­ried off, but the will of e­ven a com­mon man can­not be tak­en from him.ʼ

10:1 Confucius, in his vil­lage, looked sim­ple and sin­cere, and as if he were not a­ble to speak. When he was in the princeʼs ancestorial tem­ple, or in the court, he spoke min­ute­ly on eve­ry point, but cau­tious­ly.

10:2 When he was waiting at court, in speak­ing with the great of­fic­ers of the low­er grade, he spake free­ly, but in a straight­for­ward man­ner; in speak­ing with those of the higher grade, he did so bland­ly, but pre­cise­ly. 10:3 When the prince called him to em­ploy him in the re­cep­tion of a vis­i­tor, his coun­te­nance ap­peared to change, and his legs to move for­ward with dif­fi­cul­ty. He in­clined him­self to the oth­er of­fic­ers a­mong whom he stood, mov­ing his left or right arm, as their po­si­tion re­quired, but keep­ing the skirts of his robe be­fore and be­hind e­ven­ly ad­justed. 10:4 When he en­tered the pal­ace gate, he seemed to bend his bod­y, as if it were not suf­fi­cient to ad­mit him. When he was stand­ing, he did not oc­cu­py the mid­dle of the gate-way; when he passed in or out, he did not tread up­on the thresh­old. When he was pass­ing the va­cant place of the prince, his coun­te­nance ap­peared to change, and his legs to bend un­der him, and his words came as if he hard­ly had breath to ut­ter them. He as­cended the re­cep­tion hall, hold­ing up his robe with both his hands, and his bod­y bent; hold­ing in his breath al­so, as if he dared not breathe. 10:6 The su­pe­ri­or man did not use a deep pur­ple, or a puce colour, in the or­na­ments of his dress. E­ven in his un­dress, he did not wear any­thing of a red or red­dish colour. 12:4 Sze-ma Niu asked a­bout the su­pe­ri­or man. The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man has nei­ther anx­i­e­ty nor fear.ʼ ʼBeing with­out anx­i­e­ty or fear!ʼ said Nui;-- ʼdoes this con­sti­tute what we call the su­pe­ri­or man?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼWhen in­ter­nal ex­am­i­na­tion dis­cov­ers noth­ing wrong, what is there to be anx­ious a­bout, what is there to fear?ʼ

12:5 Sze-ma Niu, full of anx­i­e­ty, said, ʼOther men all have their broth­ers, I on­ly have not.ʼ Tsze-hsia said to him, ʼThere is the fol­low­ing say­ing which I have heard:-- ʼ"Death and life have their de­ter­mined ap­point­ment; rich­es and honours de­pend up­on Heav­en." ʼLet the su­pe­ri­or man nev­er fail rev­er­en­tial­ly to or­der his own con­duct, and let him be re­spect­ful to oth­ers and ob­ser­vant of pro­pri­e­ty:-- then all with­in the four seas will be his broth­ers. 12:6 Tsze-chang asked what con­sti­tut­ed in­tel­li­gence. The Mas­ter said, ʼHe with whom nei­ther slan­der that grad­u­al­ly soaks in­to the mind, nor state­ments that star­tle like a wound in the flesh, are suc­cess­ful, may be called in­tel­li­gent in­deed. Yea, he with whom nei­ther soaking slan­der, nor startling state­ments, are suc­cess­ful, may be called far­see­ing.ʼ

12:7 Tsze-kung asked a­bout gov­ern­ment. The Mas­ter said, ʼThe req­ui­sites of gov­ern­ment are that there be suf­fi­cien­cy of food, suf­fi­cien­cy of mil­i­tar­y e­quip­ment, and the con­fi­dence of the peo­ple in their rul­er.ʼ Tsze-kung said, ʼIf it can­not be helped, and one of these must be dis­pensed with, which of the three should be fore­gone first?ʼ ʼThe mil­i­tar­y e­quip­ment,ʼ said the Mas­ter. Tsze-kung a­gain asked, ʼIf it can­not be helped, and one of the remaining two must be dis­pensed with, which of them should be fore­gone?ʼ The Mas­ter an­swered, ʼPart with the food. From of old, death has been the lot of all men; but if the peo­ple have no faith in their rul­ers, there is no stand­ing for the state.ʼ

12:8 Chi Tsze-chʼang said, ʼIn a su­pe­ri­or man it is on­ly the sub­stan­tial qualities which are wanted;-- why should we seek for or­na­men­tal ac­com­plish­ments?ʼ Tsze-kung said, ʼAlas! Your words, sir, show you to be a su­pe­ri­or man, but four hors­es can­not o­ver­take the tongue. Or­na­ment is as sub­stance; sub­stance is as or­na­ment. The hide of a ti­ger or a leop­ard strip­ped of its hair, is like the hide of a dog or a goat strip­ped of its hair.ʼ

12:9 The Duke A­i in­quired of Yu Zo, say­ing, ʼThe year is one of scar­ci­ty, and the re­turns for ex­pend­i­ture are not suf­fi­cient;-- what is to be done?ʼ Yu Zo replied to him, ʼWhy not simp­ly tithe the peo­ple?ʼ ʼWith two tenths, said the duke, ʼI find it not e­nough;-- how could I do with that sys­tem of one tenth?ʼ Yu Zo an­swered, ʼIf the peo­ple have plen­ty, their prince will not be left to want a­lone. If the peo­ple are in want, their prince can­not en­joy plen­ty a­lone.ʼ

12:10 Tsze-chang hav­ing asked how vir­tue was to be ex­alt­ed, and de­lu­sions to be dis­cov­ered, the Mas­ter said, ʼHold faith­ful­ness and sin­cer­i­ty as first prin­ci­ples, and be mov­ing con­tin­u­al­ly to what is right;-- this is the way to ex­alt oneʼs vir­tue. ʼYou love a man and wish him to live; you hate him and wish him to die. Hav­ing wished him to live, you al­so wish him to die. This is a case of de­lu­sion. ʼ"It may not be on ac­count of her be­ing rich, yet you come to make a dif­fer­ence."ʼ

12:11 The Duke Ching, of Chʼi, asked Confucius a­bout gov­ern­ment. Confucius replied, ʼThere is gov­ern­ment, when the prince is prince, and the min­is­ter is min­is­ter; when the fa­ther is fa­ther, and the son is son.ʼ ʼGood!ʼ said the duke; ʼif, in­deed; the prince be not prince, the min­is­ter not min­is­ter, the fa­ther not fa­ther, and the son not son, al­though I have my rev­e­nue, can I en­joy it?ʼ

12:13 The Mas­ter said, ʼIn hear­ing lit­i­ga­tions, I am like any oth­er bod­y. What is nec­es­sar­y, how­ev­er, is to cause the peo­ple to have no lit­i­ga­tions.ʼ

12:14 Tsze-chang asked a­bout gov­ern­ment. The Mas­ter said, ʼThe art of governing is to keep its af­fairs be­fore the mind with­out wea­ri­ness, and to prac­tise them with un­de­vi­at­ing con­sist­en­cy.ʼ

12:15 The Mas­ter said, ʼBy ex­ten­sive­ly stud­y­ing all learn­ing, and keep­ing him­self un­der the re­straint of the rules of pro­pri­e­ty, one may thus like­wise not err from what is right.ʼ

12:16 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man seeks to per­fect the ad­mi­ra­ble qualities of men, and does not seek to per­fect their bad qualities. The mean man does the op­po­site of this.ʼ

12:17 Chi Kʼang asked Confucius a­bout gov­ern­ment. Confucius replied, ʼTo gov­ern means to rec­ti­fy. If you lead on the peo­ple with cor­rect­ness, who will dare not to be cor­rect?ʼ

12:18 Chi Kʼang, dis­tressed a­bout the num­ber of thieves in the state, in­quired of Confucius how to do a­way with them. Confucius said, ʼIf you, sir, were not cov­et­ous, al­though you should re­ward them to do it, they would not steal.ʼ

12:19 Chi Kʼang asked Confucius a­bout gov­ern­ment, say­ing, ʼWhat do you say to kill­ing the un­prin­ci­pled for the good of the prin­ci­pled?ʼ Confucius replied, ʼSir, in car­ry­ing on your gov­ern­ment, why should you use kill­ing at all? Let your e­vinced de­sires be for what is good, and the peo­ple will be good. The re­la­tion be­tween su­pe­ri­ors and in­fe­ri­ors, is like that be­tween the wind and the grass. The grass must bend, when the wind blows a­cross it.ʼ

12:20 Tsze-chang asked, ʼWhat must the of­fic­er be, who may be said to be dis­tin­guished?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼWhat is it you call be­ing dis­tin­guished?ʼ Tsze-chang replied, ʼIt is to be heard of through the State, to be heard of through­out his clan.ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼThat is notoriety, not dis­tinc­tion. ʼNow the man of dis­tinc­tion is sol­id and straight­for­ward, and loves right­eous­ness. He ex­am­ines peo­pleʼs words, and looks at their coun­te­nances. He is anx­ious to hum­ble him­self to oth­ers. Such a man will be dis­tin­guished in the coun­try; he will be dis­tin­guished in his clan. ʼAs to the man of notoriety, he as­sumes the ap­pear­ance of vir­tue, but his ac­tions are opposed to it, and he rests in this char­ac­ter with­out any doubts a­bout him­self. Such a man will be heard of in the coun­try; he will be heard of in the clan.ʼ

12:21 Fan Chʼih ram­bling with the Mas­ter un­der the trees a­bout the rain al­tars, said, ʼI ven­ture to ask how to ex­alt vir­tue, to cor­rect cher­ished evil, and to dis­cov­er de­lu­sions.ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼTruly a good ques­tion! ʼIf do­ing what is to be done be made the first busi­ness, and suc­cess a sec­ond­ar­y con­sid­er­a­tion;-- is not this the way to ex­alt vir­tue? To as­sail oneʼs own wick­ed­ness and not as­sail that of oth­ers;-- is not this the way to cor­rect cher­ished evil? For a morn­ingʼs an­ger to dis­re­gard oneʼs own life, and in­volve that of his par­ents;-- is not this a case of de­lu­sion?ʼ

12:22 Fan Chʼih asked a­bout be­nev­o­lence. The Mas­ter said, ʼIt is to love all men.ʼ He asked a­bout knowl­edge. The Mas­ter said, ʼIt is to know all men.ʼ Fan Chʼih did not im­me­di­ate­ly un­der­stand these an­swers. The Mas­ter said, ʼEmploy the up­right and put a­side all the crook­ed;-- in this way the crook­ed can be made to be up­right.ʼ Fan Chʼih re­tired, and, see­ing Tsze-hsia, he said to him, ʼA Lit­tle while a­go, I had an in­ter­view with our Mas­ter, and asked him a­bout knowl­edge. He said, ʼEmploy the up­right, and put a­side all the crook­ed;-- in this way, the crook­ed will be made to be up­right.ʼ What did he mean?ʼ Tsze-hsia said, ʼTruly rich is his say­ing! ʼShun, be­ing in pos­ses­sion of the king­dom, se­lected from a­mong all the peo­ple, and em­ployed Kao-yao, on which all who were de­void of vir­tue dis­ap­peared. Tʼang, be­ing in pos­ses­sion of the king­dom, se­lected from a­mong all the peo­ple, and em­ployed I Yin, and all who were de­void of vir­tue dis­ap­peared.ʼ

12:23 Tsze-kung asked a­bout friend­ship. The Mas­ter said, ʼFaithfully ad­mon­ish your friend, and skill­ful­ly lead him on. If you find him im­prac­ti­ca­ble, stop. Do not dis­grace your­self.ʼ

12:24 The phi­los­o­pher Tsang said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man on grounds of cul­ture meets with his friends, and by their friend­ship helps his vir­tue.ʼ

13:1 Tsze-lu asked a­bout gov­ern­ment. The Mas­ter said, ʼGo be­fore the peo­ple with your ex­am­ple, and be la­bo­ri­ous in their af­fairs.ʼ He re­quested fur­ther in­struc­tion, and was an­swered, ʼBe not wea­ry (in these things).ʼ

13:2 Chung-kung, be­ing chief min­is­ter to the Head of the Chi fam­i­ly, asked a­bout gov­ern­ment. The Mas­ter said, ʼEmploy first the ser­vic­es of your var­i­ous of­fic­ers, par­don small faults, and raise to of­fice men of vir­tue and tal­ents.ʼ Chung-kung said, ʼHow shall I know the men of vir­tue and tal­ent, so that I may raise them to of­fice?ʼ He was an­swered, ʼRaise to of­fice those whom you know. As to those whom you do not know, will oth­ers ne­glect them?ʼ

13:3 Tsze-lu said, ʼThe rul­er of Wei has been waiting for you, in or­der with you to ad­min­is­ter the gov­ern­ment. What will you con­sid­er the first thing to be done?ʼ The Mas­ter replied, ʼWhat is nec­es­sar­y is to rec­ti­fy names.ʼ ʼSo, in­deed!ʼ said Tsze-lu. ʼYou are wide of the mark! Why must there be such rec­ti­fi­ca­tion?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼHow un­cul­ti­vat­ed you are, Yu! A su­pe­ri­or man, in re­gard to what he does not know, shows a cau­tious re­serve. ʼIf names be not cor­rect, lan­guage is not in ac­cord­ance with the truth of things. If lan­guage be not in ac­cord­ance with the truth of things, af­fairs can­not be car­ried on to suc­cess. ʼWhen af­fairs can­not be car­ried on to suc­cess, proprieties and mu­sic will not flour­ish. When proprieties and mu­sic do not flour­ish, pun­ish­ments will not be prop­er­ly a­warded. When pun­ish­ments are not prop­er­ly a­warded, the peo­ple do not know how to move hand or foot. ʼTherefore a su­pe­ri­or man con­sid­ers it nec­es­sar­y that the names he uses may be spo­ken ap­pro­pri­ate­ly, and al­so that what he speaks may be car­ried out ap­pro­pri­ate­ly. What the su­pe­ri­or man re­quires, is just that in his words there may be noth­ing in­cor­rect.ʼ

13:4 Fan Chʼih re­quested to be taught hus­band­ry. The Mas­ter said, ʼI am not so good for that as an old hus­band­man.ʼ He re­quested al­so to be taught gar­den­ing, and was an­swered, ʼI am not so good for that as an old gar­den­er.ʼ Fan Chʼih hav­ing gone out, the Mas­ter said, ʼA small man, in­deed, is Fan Hsu! If a su­pe­ri­or love pro­pri­e­ty, the peo­ple will not dare not to be rev­er­ent. If he love right­eous­ness, the peo­ple will not dare not to sub­mit to his ex­am­ple. If he love good faith, the peo­ple will not dare not to be sin­cere. Now, when these things ob­tain, the peo­ple from all quar­ters will come to him, bear­ing their chil­dren on their backs;-- what need has he of a knowl­edge of hus­band­ry?ʼ

13:5 The Mas­ter said, ʼThough a man may be a­ble to re­cite the three hun­dred odes, yet if, when in­trusted with a gov­ern­men­tal charge, he knows not how to act, or if, when sent to any quar­ter on a mis­sion, he can­not give his replies un­as­sist­ed, not­with­stand­ing the ex­tent of his learn­ing, of what prac­ti­cal use is it?ʼ

13:6 The Mas­ter said, ʼWhen a princeʼs per­son­al con­duct is cor­rect, his gov­ern­ment is ef­fec­tive with­out the is­su­ing of or­ders. If his per­son­al con­duct is not cor­rect, he may is­sue or­ders, but they will not be fol­lowed.ʼ

13:7 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe gov­ern­ments of Lu and Wei are broth­ers.ʼ

13:8 The Mas­ter said of Ching, a sci­on of the du­cal fam­i­ly of Wei, that he knew the e­con­o­my of a fam­i­ly well. When he be­gan to have means, he said, ʼHa! here is a col­lec­tion!ʼ When they were a lit­tle in­creased, he said, ʼHa! this is com­plete!ʼ When he had be­come rich, he said, ʼHa! this is ad­mi­ra­ble!ʼ

The Mas­ter ob­served, ʼHow nu­mer­ous are the peo­ple!ʼ Yu said, ʼSince they are thus nu­mer­ous, what more shall be done for them?ʼ ʼEnrich them,ʼ was the re­ply. ʼAnd when they have been en­riched, what more shall be done?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼTeach them.ʼ

13:10 The Mas­ter said, ʼIf there were (any of the prin­ces) who would em­ploy me, in the course of twelve months, I should have done some­thing con­sid­er­a­ble. In three years, the gov­ern­ment would be per­fected.ʼ

13:11 The Mas­ter said, ʼ"If good men were to gov­ern a coun­try in suc­ces­sion for a hun­dred years, they would be a­ble to trans­form the violently bad, and dis­pense with cap­i­tal pun­ish­ments." True in­deed is this say­ing!ʼ

13:12 The Mas­ter said, ʼIf a tru­ly roy­al rul­er were to a­rise, it would still re­quire a gen­er­a­tion, and then vir­tue would pre­vail.ʼ

13:13 The Mas­ter said, ʼIf a min­is­ter make his own con­duct cor­rect, what dif­fi­cul­ty will he have in assisting in gov­ern­ment? If he can­not rec­ti­fy him­self, what has he to do with rec­ti­fy­ing oth­ers?ʼ

13:14 The dis­ci­ple Zan returning from the court, the Mas­ter said to him, ʼHow are you so late?ʼ He replied, ʼWe had gov­ern­ment busi­ness.ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼIt must have been fam­i­ly af­fairs. If there had been gov­ern­ment busi­ness, though I am not now in of­fice, I should have been con­sulted a­bout it.ʼ

13:15 The Duke Ting asked wheth­er there was a sin­gle sen­tence which could make a coun­try pros­per­ous. Confucius replied, ʼSuch an ef­fect can­not be ex­pected from one sen­tence. ʼThere is a say­ing, how­ev­er, which peo­ple have-- "To be a prince is dif­fi­cult; to be a min­is­ter is not eas­y." ʼIf a rul­er knows this,-- the dif­fi­cul­ty of be­ing a prince,-- may there not be ex­pected from this one sen­tence the pros­per­i­ty of his coun­try?ʼ The duke then said, ʼIs there a sin­gle sen­tence which can ru­in a coun­try?ʼ Confucius replied, ʼSuch an ef­fect as that can­not be ex­pected from one sen­tence. There is, how­ev­er, the say­ing which peo­ple have-- "I have no pleas­ure in be­ing a prince, but on­ly in that no one can of­fer any op­po­si­tion to what I say!" ʼIf a rul­erʼs words be good, is it not al­so good that no one op­pose them? But if they are not good, and no one op­poses them, may there not be ex­pected from this one sen­tence the ru­in of his coun­try?ʼ

13:16 The Duke of Sheh asked a­bout gov­ern­ment. The Mas­ter said, ʼGood gov­ern­ment ob­tains, when those who are near are made hap­py, and those who are far off are at­tracted.ʼ

13:17 Tsze-hsia, be­ing gov­er­nor of Chu-fu, asked a­bout gov­ern­ment. The Mas­ter said, ʼDo not be de­sir­ous to have things done quick­ly; do not look at small ad­van­tages. De­sire to have things done quick­ly pre­vents their be­ing done thor­ough­ly. Looking at small ad­van­tages pre­vents great af­fairs from be­ing ac­com­plished.ʼ

13:18 The Duke of Sheh in­formed Confucius, say­ing, ʼAmong us here there are those who may be styled up­right in their con­duct. If their fa­ther have stol­en a sheep, they will bear wit­ness to the fact.ʼ Confucius said, ʼAmong us, in our part of the coun­try, those who are up­right are dif­fer­ent from this. The fa­ther con­ceals the mis­con­duct of the son, and the son con­ceals the mis­con­duct of the fa­ther. Uprightness is to be found in this.ʼ

13:19 Fan Chʼih asked a­bout per­fect vir­tue. The Mas­ter said, ʼIt is, in re­tire­ment, to be se­date­ly gra­ve; in the man­age­ment of busi­ness, to be reverently at­ten­tive; in in­ter­course with oth­ers, to be strict­ly sin­cere. Though a man go a­mong rude, un­cul­ti­vat­ed tribes, these qualities may not be ne­glected.ʼ

13:20 Tsze-kung asked, say­ing, ʼWhat qualities must a man pos­sess to en­ti­tle him to be called an of­fic­er? The Mas­ter said, ʼHe who in his con­duct of him­self main­tains a sense of shame, and when sent to any quar­ter will not dis­grace his princeʼs com­mis­sion, de­serves to be called an of­fic­er.ʼ Tsze-kung pur­sued, ʼI ven­ture to ask who may be placed in the next low­er rank?ʼ And he was told, ʼHe whom the cir­cle of his rel­a­tives pro­nounce to be fil­ial, whom his fel­low-villagers and neigh­bours pro­nounce to be fra­ter­nal.ʼ A­gain the dis­ci­ple asked, ʼI ven­ture to ask a­bout the class still next in or­der.ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼThey are de­ter­mined to be sin­cere in what they say, and to car­ry out what they do. They are ob­sti­nate lit­tle men. Yet per­haps they may make the next class.ʼ Tsze-kung fi­nal­ly in­quired, ʼOf what sort are those of the pres­ent day, who en­gage in gov­ern­ment?ʼ The Mas­ter said ʼPooh! they are so man­y pecks and ham­pers, not worth be­ing tak­en in­to ac­count.ʼ

13:21 The Mas­ter said, ʼSince I can­not get men pur­su­ing the due me­di­um, to whom I might com­mu­ni­cate my in­struc­tions, I must find the ar­dent and the cau­tious­ly-decided. The ar­dent will ad­vance and lay hold of truth; the cau­tious­ly-decided will keep them­selves from what is wrong.ʼ

13:22 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe peo­ple of the south have a say­ing-- "A man with­out con­stan­cy can­not be ei­ther a wiz­ard or a doc­tor." Good! ʼInconstant in his vir­tue, he will be vis­ited with dis­grace.ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼThis a­rises simp­ly from not at­tend­ing to the prog­nos­ti­ca­tion.ʼ

13:23 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man is af­fa­ble, but not ad­u­la­to­ry; the mean man is ad­u­la­to­ry, but not af­fa­ble.ʼ

13:24 Tsze-kung asked, say­ing, ʼWhat do you say of a man who is loved by all the peo­ple of his neigh­bor­hood?ʼ The Mas­ter replied, ʼWe may not for that ac­cord our ap­prov­al of him.ʼ ʼAnd what do you say of him who is hat­ed by all the peo­ple of his neigh­bor­hood?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼWe may not for that con­clude that he is bad. It is bet­ter than ei­ther of these cases that the good in the neigh­bor­hood love him, and the bad hate him.ʼ

13:25 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man is eas­y to serve and dif­fi­cult to please. If you try to please him in any way which is not ac­cord­ant with right, he will not be pleased. But in his em­ploy­ment of men, he uses them ac­cord­ing to their ca­pac­i­ty. The mean man is dif­fi­cult to serve, and eas­y to please. If you try to please him, though it be in a way which is not ac­cord­ant with right, he may be pleased. But in his em­ploy­ment of men, he wishes them to be e­qual to eve­ry­thing.ʼ

13:26 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man has a dig­ni­fied ease with­out pride. The mean man has pride with­out a dig­ni­fied ease.ʼ

13:27 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe firm, the en­dur­ing, the sim­ple, and the mod­est are near to vir­tue.ʼ

13:28 Tsze-lu asked, say­ing, ʼWhat qualities must a man pos­sess to en­ti­tle him to be called a schol­ar?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼHe must be thus,-- ear­nest, ur­gent, and bland:-- a­mong his friends, ear­nest and ur­gent; a­mong his breth­ren, bland.ʼ

13:29 The Mas­ter said, ʼLet a good man teach the peo­ple sev­en years, and they may then like­wise be em­ployed in war.ʼ

14:1 Hsien asked what was shame­ful. The Mas­ter said, ʼWhen good gov­ern­ment pre­vails in a state, to be think­ing on­ly of sal­a­ry; and, when bad gov­ern­ment pre­vails, to be think­ing, in the same way, on­ly of sal­a­ry;-- this is shame­ful.ʼ

14:2 ʼWhen the love of su­pe­ri­or­i­ty, boasting, re­sent­ments, and cov­et­ous­ness are re­pressed, this may be deemed per­fect vir­tue.ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼThis may be re­garded as the a­chieve­ment of what is dif­fi­cult. But I do not know that it is to be deemed per­fect vir­tue.ʼ

14:3 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe schol­ar who cherishes the love of com­fort is not fit to be deemed a schol­ar.ʼ

14:4 The Mas­ter said, ʼWhen good gov­ern­ment pre­vails in a state, lan­guage may be loft­y and bold, and ac­tions the same. When bad gov­ern­ment pre­vails, the ac­tions may be loft­y and bold, but the lan­guage may be with some re­serve.ʼ

14:5 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe vir­tu­ous will be sure to speak cor­rect­ly, but those whose speech is good may not al­ways be vir­tu­ous. Men of prin­ci­ple are sure to be bold, but those who are bold may not al­ways be men of prin­ci­ple.ʼ

14:6 Nan-kung Kwo, sub­mit­ting an in­quir­y to Confucius, said, ʼI was skill­ful at ar­cher­y, and Ao could move a boat a­long up­on the land, but nei­ther of them died a nat­u­ral death. Yu and Chi per­son­al­ly wrought at the toils of hus­band­ry, and they be­came pos­ses­sors of the king­dom.ʼ The Mas­ter made no re­ply; but when Nan-kung Kwo went out, he said, ʼA su­pe­ri­or man in­deed is this! An esteemer of vir­tue in­deed is this!ʼ

14:7 The Mas­ter said, ʼSuperior men, and yet not al­ways vir­tu­ous, there have been, a­las! But there nev­er has been a mean man, and, at the same time, vir­tu­ous.ʼ

14:8 The Mas­ter said, ʼCan there be love which does not lead to strictness with its ob­ject? Can there be loy­al­ty which does not lead to the in­struc­tion of its ob­ject?ʼ

14:9 The Mas­ter said, ʼIn pre­par­ing the gov­ern­men­tal no­ti­fi­ca­tions, Pʼi Shan first made the rough draft; Shi-shu ex­am­ined and dis­cussed its con­tents; Tsze-yu, the man­ag­er of For­eign in­ter­course, then pol­ished the style; and, fi­nal­ly, Tsze-chʼan of Tung-li gave it the prop­er el­e­gance and fin­ish.ʼ

14:10 Some one asked a­bout Tsze-chʼan. The Mas­ter said, ʼHe was a kind man.ʼ He asked a­bout Tsze-hsi. The Mas­ter said, ʼThat man! That man!ʼ He asked a­bout Kwan Chung. ʼFor him,ʼ said the Mas­ter, ʼthe cit­y of Pien, with three hun­dred families, was tak­en from the chief of the Po fam­i­ly, who did not ut­ter a murmuring word, though, to the end of his life, he had on­ly coarse rice to eat.ʼ

14:11 The Mas­ter said, ʼTo be poor with­out murmuring is dif­fi­cult. To be rich with­out be­ing proud is eas­y.ʼ

14:12 The Mas­ter said, ʼMang Kung-chʼo is more than fit to be chief of­fic­er in the families of Chao and Wei, but he is not fit to be great of­fic­er to ei­ther of the States Tang or Hsieh.ʼ

14:13 Tsze-lu asked what con­sti­tut­ed a COMPLETE man. The Mas­ter said, ʼSuppose a man with the knowl­edge of Tsang Wu-chung, the free­dom from cov­et­ous­ness of Kung-chʼo, the brav­er­y of Chwang of Pien, and the var­ied tal­ents of Zan Chʼiu; add to these the ac­com­plish­ments of the rules of pro­pri­e­ty and mu­sic:-- such a one might be reck­oned a COMPLETE man.ʼ He then added, ʼBut what is the ne­ces­si­ty for a com­plete man of the pres­ent day to have all these things? The man, who in the view of gain, thinks of right­eous­ness; who in the view of dan­ger is pre­pared to give up his life; and who does not for­get an old a­gree­ment how­ev­er far back it ex­tends:-- such a man may be reck­oned a COMPLETE man.ʼ

14:14 The Mas­ter asked Kung-ming Chia a­bout Kung- shu Wan, say­ing, ʼIs it true that your mas­ter speaks not, laughs not, and takes not?ʼ Kung-ming Chia replied, ʼThis has a­ris­en from the re­port­ers go­ing be­yond the truth.-- My mas­ter speaks when it is the time to speak, and so men do not get tired of his speak­ing. He laughs when there is oc­ca­sion to be joy­ful, and so men do not get tired of his laugh­ing. He takes when it is con­sist­ent with right­eous­ness to do so, and so men do not get tired of his tak­ing.ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼSo! But is it so with him?ʼ

14:15 The Mas­ter said, ʼTsang Wu-chung, keep­ing pos­ses­sion of Fang, asked of the duke of Lu to ap­point a suc­ces­sor to him in his fam­i­ly. Al­though it may be said that he was not us­ing force with his sov­er­eign, I be­lieve he was.ʼ

14:16 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe duke Wan of Tsin was craft­y and not up­right. The duke Hwan of Chʼi was up­right and not craft­y.ʼ

14:17 Tsze-lu said, ʼThe Duke Hwan caused his broth­er Chiu to be killed, when Shao Hu died with his mas­ter, but Kwan Chung did not die. May not I say that he was want­ing in vir­tue?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼThe Duke Hwan as­sem­bled all the prin­ces to­geth­er, and that not with weap­ons of war and char­i­ots:-- it was all through the in­flu­ence of Kwan Chung. Whose be­nef­i­cence was like his? Whose be­nef­i­cence was like his?ʼ

14:18 Tsze-kung said, ʼKwan Chung, I ap­pre­hend, was want­ing in vir­tue. When the Duke Hwan caused his broth­er Chiu to be killed, Kwan Chung was not a­ble to die with him. More­o­ver, he be­came prime min­is­ter to Hwan.ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼKwan Chung acted as prime min­is­ter to the Duke Hwan, made him lead­er of all the prin­ces, and u­nit­ed and rec­ti­fied the whole king­dom. Down to the pres­ent day, the peo­ple en­joy the gifts which he con­ferred. But for Kwan Chung, we should now be wear­ing our hair un­bound, and the lap­pets of our coats buttoning on the left side. ʼWill you re­quire from him the small fi­del­i­ty of com­mon men and com­mon wom­en, who would com­mit su­i­cide in a stream or ditch, no one know­ing any­thing a­bout them?ʼ

14:19 The great of­fic­er, Hsien, who had been fam­i­ly-minister to Kung-shu Wan, as­cended to the princeʼs court in com­pa­ny with Wan. The Mas­ter, hav­ing heard of it, said, ʼHe de­served to be con­sid­ered WAN (the ac­com­plished).ʼ

14:20 The Mas­ter was speak­ing a­bout the un­prin­ci­pled course of the duke Ling of Wei, when Chʼi Kʼang said, ʼSince he is of such a char­ac­ter, how is it he does not lose his State?ʼ Confucius said, ʼThe Chung-shu Yu has the su­per­in­tend­ence of his guests and of stran­gers; the litanist, Tʼo, has the man­age­ment of his an­ces­tral tem­ple; and Wang-sun Chia has the di­rec­tion of the ar­my and forces:-- with such of­fic­ers as these, how should he lose his State?ʼ

14:21 The Mas­ter said, ʼHe who speaks with­out mod­es­ty will find it dif­fi­cult to make his words good.ʼ

14:22 Chan Chʼang mur­dered the Duke Chien of Chʼi. Confucius bathed, went to court, and in­formed the duke A­i, say­ing, ʼChan Hang has slain his sov­er­eign. I beg that you will un­der­take to pun­ish him.ʼ The duke said, ʼInform the chiefs of the three families of it.ʼ Confucius re­tired, and said, ʼFollowing in the rear of the great of­fic­ers, I did not dare not to rep­re­sent such a mat­ter, and my prince says, "Inform the chiefs of the three families of it."ʼ He went to the chiefs, and in­formed them, but they would not act. Confucius then said, ʼFollowing in the rear of the great of­fic­ers, I did not dare not to rep­re­sent such a mat­ter.ʼ

14:23 Tsze-lu asked how a rul­er should be served. The Mas­ter said, ʼDo not im­pose on him, and, more­o­ver, with­stand him to his face.ʼ

14:24 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe prog­ress of the su­pe­ri­or man is up­wards; the prog­ress of the mean man is down­wards.ʼ

14:25 The Mas­ter said, ʼIn an­cient times, men learn­ed with a view to their own im­prove­ment. Now-a-days, men learn with a view to the ap­pro­ba­tion of oth­ers.ʼ

14:26 Chu Po-yu sent a mes­sen­ger with friend­ly in­quir­ies to Confucius. Confucius sat with him, and ques­tioned him. ʼWhat,ʼ said he, ʼis your mas­ter en­gaged in?ʼ The mes­sen­ger replied, ʼMy mas­ter is anx­ious to make his faults few, but he has not yet suc­ceeded.ʼ He then went out, and the Mas­ter said, ʼA mes­sen­ger in­deed! A mes­sen­ger in­deed!ʼ

14:27 The Mas­ter said, ʼHe who is not in any par­tic­u­lar of­fice, has noth­ing to do with plans for the ad­min­is­tra­tion of its duties.ʼ

14:28 The phi­los­o­pher Tsang said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man, in his thoughts, does not go out of his place.ʼ

14:29 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man is mod­est in his speech, but ex­ceeds in his ac­tions.ʼ

14:30 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe way of the su­pe­ri­or man is three­fold, but I am not e­qual to it. Vir­tu­ous, he is free from anxieties; wise, he is free from perplexities; bold, he is free from fear. Tsze-kung said, ʼMaster, that is what you your­self say.ʼ

14:31 Tsze-kung was in the hab­it of com­par­ing men to­geth­er. The Mas­ter said, ʼTsze must have reached a high pitch of ex­cel­lence! Now, I have not lei­sure for this.ʼ

14:32 The Mas­ter said, ʼI will not be con­cerned at menʼs not know­ing me; I will be con­cerned at my own want of a­bil­i­ty.ʼ

14:33 The Mas­ter said, ʼHe who does not an­tic­i­pate at­tempts to de­ceive him, nor think be­fore­hand of his not be­ing be­lieved, and yet ap­pre­hends these things read­i­ly (when they oc­cur);-- is he not a man of su­pe­ri­or worth?ʼ

14:34 Wei-shang Mau said to Confucius, ʼChʼiu, how is it that you keep roosting a­bout? Is it not that you are an in­sin­u­at­ing talk­er?ʼ Confucius said, ʼI do not dare to play the part of such a talk­er, but I hate ob­sti­na­cy.ʼ

14:35 The Mas­ter said, ʼA horse is called a chʼi, not be­cause of its strength, but be­cause of its oth­er good qualities.ʼ

14:36 Some one said, ʼWhat do you say con­cern­ing the prin­ci­ple that in­ju­ry should be rec­om­pensed with kind­ness?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼWith what then will you rec­om­pense kind­ness? ʼRecompense in­ju­ry with jus­tice, and rec­om­pense kind­ness with kind­ness.ʼ

14:37 The Mas­ter said, ʼAlas! there is no one that knows me.ʼ Tsze-kung said, ʼWhat do you mean by thus say­ing-- that no one knows you?ʼ The Mas­ter replied, ʼI do not mur­mur a­gainst Heav­en. I do not grum­ble a­gainst men. My stud­ies lie low, and my pen­e­tra­tion rises high. But there is Heav­en;-- that knows me!ʼ

14:38 The Kung-po Liao, hav­ing slan­dered Tsze-lu to Chi-sun, Tsze-fu Ching-po in­formed Confucius of it, say­ing, ʼOur mas­ter is cer­tain­ly be­ing led a­stray by the Kung-po Liao, but I have still pow­er e­nough left to cut Liao off, and ex­pose his corpse in the mar­ket and in the court.ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼIf my prin­ci­ples are to ad­vance, it is so or­dered. If they are to fall to the ground, it is so or­dered. What can the Kung-po Liao do where such ordering is con­cerned?ʼ

14:39 The Mas­ter said, ʼSome men of worth re­tire from the world. Some re­tire from par­tic­u­lar states. Some re­tire be­cause of dis­re­spect­ful looks. Some re­tire be­cause of con­tra­dic­to­ry lan­guage.ʼ

14:40 The Mas­ter said, ʼThose who have done this are sev­en men.ʼ

14:41 Tsze-lu hap­pen­ing to pass the night in Shih-man, the gate­keep­er said to him, ʼWhom do you come from?ʼ Tsze-lu said, ʼFrom Mr. Kʼung.ʼ ʼIt is he,-- is it not?ʼ-- said the oth­er, ʼwho knows the im­prac­ti­ca­ble na­ture of the times and yet will be do­ing in them.ʼ

14:42 The Mas­ter was playing, one day, on a mu­si­cal stone in Wei, when a man, car­ry­ing a straw bas­ket, passed the door of the house where Confucius was, and said, ʼHis heart is full who so beats the mu­si­cal stone.ʼ A lit­tle while af­ter, he added, ʼHow con­tempt­i­ble is the one-ideaed ob­sti­na­cy those sounds dis­play! When one is tak­en no no­tice of, he has simp­ly at once to give o­ver his wish for pub­lic em­ploy­ment. "Deep wa­ter must be crossed with the clothes on; shal­low wa­ter may be crossed with the clothes held up."ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼHow de­ter­mined is he in his pur­pose! But this is not dif­fi­cult!ʼ

14:43 Tsze-chang said, ʼWhat is meant when the Shu says that Kao-tsung, while ob­serv­ing the u­su­al im­pe­ri­al mourn­ing, was for three years with­out speak­ing?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼWhy must Kao-tsung be re­ferred to as an ex­am­ple of this? The an­cients all did so. When the sov­er­eign died, the of­fic­ers all at­tended to their sev­er­al duties, tak­ing in­struc­tions from the prime min­is­ter for three years.ʼ

14:44 The Mas­ter said, ʼWhen rul­ers love to ob­serve the rules of pro­pri­e­ty, the peo­ple re­spond read­i­ly to the calls on them for serv­ice.ʼ

14:45 Tsze-lu asked what con­sti­tut­ed the su­pe­ri­or man. The Mas­ter said, ʼThe cul­ti­va­tion of him­self in rev­er­en­tial care­ful­ness.ʼ ʼAnd is this all?ʼ said Tsze-lu. ʼHe cul­ti­vates him­self so as to give rest to oth­ers,ʼ was the re­ply. ʼAnd is this all?ʼ a­gain asked Tsze-lu. The Mas­ter said, ʼHe cul­ti­vates him­self so as to give rest to all the peo­ple. He cul­ti­vates him­self so as to give rest to all the peo­ple:-- e­ven Yao and Shun were still so­lic­i­tous a­bout this.ʼ

14:46 Yu­an Zang was squat­ting on his heels, and so waited the ap­proach of the Mas­ter, who said to him, ʼIn youth not hum­ble as be­fits a jun­ior; in man­hood, do­ing noth­ing wor­thy of be­ing hand­ed down; and liv­ing on to old age:-- this is to be a pest.ʼ With this he hit him on the shank with his staff.

14:47 A youth of the vil­lage of Chʼueh was em­ployed by Confucius to car­ry the mes­sages be­tween him and his vis­i­tors. Some one asked a­bout him, say­ing, ʼI sup­pose he has made great prog­ress.ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼI ob­serve that he is fond of occupying the seat of a full-grown man; I ob­serve that he walks shoul­der to shoul­der with his el­ders. He is not one who is seek­ing to make prog­ress in learn­ing. He wishes quick­ly to be­come a man.ʼ

Book 15

15:1 The Duke Ling of Wei asked Confucius a­bout tac­tics. Confucius replied, ʼI have heard all a­bout sac­ri­fi­cial ves­sels, but I have not learn­ed mil­i­tar­y mat­ters.ʼ On this, he took his de­par­ture the next day. When he was in Chan, their pro­vi­sions were ex­hausted, and his fol­low­ers be­came so ill that they were un­a­ble to rise. Tsze-lu, with ev­i­dent dis­sat­is­fac­tion, said, ʼHas the su­pe­ri­or man like­wise to en­dure in this way?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man may in­deed have to en­dure want, but the mean man, when he is in want, gives way to un­bri­dled li­cense.ʼ

15:2 The Mas­ter said, ʼTsʼze, you think, I sup­pose, that I am one who learns man­y things and keeps them in mem­o­ry?ʼ Tsze-kung replied, ʼYes,-- but per­haps it is not so?ʼ ʼNo,ʼ was the an­swer; ʼI seek a u­ni­ty all-pervading.ʼ

15:3 The Mas­ter said, ʼYu, those who know vir­tue are few.ʼ

15:4 The Mas­ter said, ʼMay not Shun be in­stanced as hav­ing gov­erned ef­fi­cient­ly with­out ex­er­tion? What did he do? He did noth­ing but grave­ly and reverently oc­cu­py his roy­al seat.ʼ

15:5 Tsze-chang asked how a man should con­duct him­self, so as to be eve­ry­where ap­pre­ci­at­ed. The Mas­ter said, ʼLet his words be sin­cere and truth­ful, and his ac­tions honourable and care­ful;-- such con­duct may be prac­tised a­mong the rude tribes of the South or the North. If his words be not sin­cere and truth­ful and his ac­tions not honourable and care­ful, will he, with such con­duct, be ap­pre­ci­at­ed, e­ven in his neigh­bor­hood? ʼWhen he is stand­ing, let him see those two things, as it were, fronting him. When he is in a car­riage, let him see them at­tached to the yoke. Then may he sub­se­quent­ly car­ry them in­to prac­tice.ʼ Tsze-chang wrote these coun­sels on the end of his sash.

15:6 The Mas­ter said, ʼTruly straight­for­ward was the his­to­ri­og­ra­pher Yu. When good gov­ern­ment pre­vailed in his State, he was like an ar­row. When bad gov­ern­ment pre­vailed, he was like an ar­row. A su­pe­ri­or man in­deed is Chu Po-yu! When good gov­ern­ment pre­vails in his state, he is to be found in of­fice. When bad gov­ern­ment pre­vails, he can roll his prin­ci­ples up, and keep them in his breast.ʼ

15:7 The Mas­ter said, ʼWhen a man may be spo­ken with, not to speak to him is to err in ref­er­ence to the man. When a man may not be spo­ken with, to speak to him is to err in ref­er­ence to our words. The wise err nei­ther in re­gard to their man nor to their words.ʼ

15:8 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe de­ter­mined schol­ar and the man of vir­tue will not seek to live at the ex­pense of in­jur­ing their vir­tue. They will e­ven sac­ri­fice their lives to pre­serve their vir­tue com­plete.ʼ

15:9 Tsze-kung asked a­bout the prac­tice of vir­tue. The Mas­ter said, ʼThe me­chan­ic, who wishes to do his work well, must first sharp­en his tools. When you are liv­ing in any state, take serv­ice with the most wor­thy a­mong its great of­fic­ers, and make friends of the most vir­tu­ous a­mong its schol­ars.ʼ

15:10 Yen Yu­an asked how the gov­ern­ment of a coun­try should be ad­min­is­tered. The Mas­ter said, ʼFollow the sea­sons of Hsia. ʼRide in the state car­riage of Yin. ʼWear the cer­e­mo­ni­al cap of Chau. ʼLet the mu­sic be the Shao with its pan­to­mimes. Ban­ish the songs of Chang, and keep far from spe­cious talk­ers. The songs of Chang are li­cen­tious; spe­cious talk­ers are dan­ger­ous.ʼ

15:11 The Mas­ter said, ʼIf a man take no thought a­bout what is dis­tant, he will find sor­row near at hand.ʼ

15:12 The Mas­ter said, ʼIt is all o­ver! I have not seen one who loves vir­tue as he loves beau­ty.ʼ

15:13 The Mas­ter said, ʼWas not Tsang Wan like one who had stol­en his sit­u­a­tion? He knew the vir­tue and the tal­ents of Hui of Liu-hsia, and yet did not pro­cure that he should stand with him in court.ʼ

15:14 The Mas­ter said, ʼHe who re­quires much from him­self and lit­tle from oth­ers, will keep him­self from be­ing the ob­ject of re­sent­ment.ʼ

15:15 The Mas­ter said, ʼWhen a man is not in the hab­it of say­ing-- "What shall I think of this? What shall I think of this?" I can in­deed do noth­ing with him!ʼ

15:16 The Mas­ter said, ʼWhen a num­ber of peo­ple are to­geth­er, for a whole day, with­out their con­ver­sa­tion turn­ing on right­eous­ness, and when they are fond of car­ry­ing out the sug­ges­tions of a small shrewd­ness;-- theirs is in­deed a hard case.ʼ

15:17 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man in eve­ry­thing con­sid­ers right­eous­ness to be es­sen­tial. He per­forms it ac­cord­ing to the rules of pro­pri­e­ty. He brings it forth in hu­mil­i­ty. He com­pletes it with sin­cer­i­ty. This is in­deed a su­pe­ri­or man.ʼ

15:18 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man is dis­tressed by his want of a­bil­i­ty. He is not dis­tressed by menʼs not know­ing him.ʼ

15:19 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man dis­likes the thought of his name not be­ing men­tioned af­ter his death.ʼ

15:20 The Mas­ter said, ʼWhat the su­pe­ri­or man seeks, is in him­self. What the mean man seeks, is in oth­ers.ʼ

15:21 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man is dig­ni­fied, but does not wran­gle. He is so­cia­ble, but not a par­ti­zan.ʼ

15:22 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man does not pro­mote a man simp­ly on ac­count of his words, nor does he put a­side good words be­cause of the man.ʼ

15:23 Tsze-kung asked, say­ing, ʼIs there one word which may serve as a rule of prac­tice for all oneʼs life?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼIs not RECIPROCITY such a word? What you do not want done to your­self, do not do to oth­ers.ʼ

15:24 The Mas­ter said, ʼIn my deal­ings with men, whose evil do I blame, whose good­ness do I praise, be­yond what is prop­er? If I do some­times ex­ceed in praise, there must be ground for it in my ex­am­i­na­tion of the in­di­vid­u­al. ʼThis peo­ple sup­plied the ground why the three dynasties pur­sued the path of straight­for­ward­ness.ʼ

15:25 The Mas­ter said, ʼEven in my ear­ly days, a his­to­ri­og­ra­pher would leave a blank in his text, and he who had a horse would lend him to an­oth­er to ride. Now, a­las! there are no such things.ʼ

15:26 The Mas­ter said, ʼSpecious words con­found vir­tue. Want of for­bear­ance in small mat­ters con­founds great plans.ʼ

15:27 The Mas­ter said, ʼWhen the mul­ti­tude hate a man, it is nec­es­sar­y to ex­am­ine in­to the case. When the mul­ti­tude like a man, it is nec­es­sar­y to ex­am­ine in­to the case.ʼ

15:28 The Mas­ter said, ʼA man can en­large the prin­ci­ples which he fol­lows; those prin­ci­ples do not en­large the man.ʼ

15:29 The Mas­ter said, ʼTo have faults and not to re­form them,-- this, in­deed, should be pro­nounced hav­ing faults.ʼ

15:30 The Mas­ter said, ʼI have been the whole day with­out eat­ing, and the whole night with­out sleep­ing:-- occupied with think­ing. It was of no use. The bet­ter plan is to learn.ʼ

15:31 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe ob­ject of the su­pe­ri­or man is truth. Food is not his ob­ject. There is plowing;-- e­ven in that there is some­times want. So with learn­ing;-- e­mol­u­ment may be found in it. The su­pe­ri­or man is anx­ious lest he should not get truth; he is not anx­ious lest pov­er­ty should come up­on him.ʼ

15:32 The Mas­ter said, ʼWhen a manʼs knowl­edge is suf­fi­cient to at­tain, and his vir­tue is not suf­fi­cient to en­a­ble him to hold, what­ev­er he may have gained, he will lose a­gain. ʼWhen his knowl­edge is suf­fi­cient to at­tain, and he has vir­tue e­nough to hold fast, if he can­not gov­ern with dig­ni­ty, the peo­ple will not re­spect him. ʼWhen his knowl­edge is suf­fi­cient to at­tain, and he has vir­tue e­nough to hold fast; when he gov­erns al­so with dig­ni­ty, yet if he try to move the peo­ple con­tra­ry to the rules of pro­pri­e­ty:-- full ex­cel­lence is not reached.ʼ

15:33 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man can­not be known in lit­tle mat­ters; but he may be in­trusted with great con­cerns. The small man may not be in­trusted with great con­cerns, but he may be known in lit­tle mat­ters.ʼ

15:34 The Mas­ter said, ʼVirtue is more to man than ei­ther wa­ter or fire. I have seen men die from tread­ing on wa­ter and fire, but I have nev­er seen a man die from tread­ing the course of vir­tue.ʼ

15:35 The Mas­ter said, ʼLet eve­ry man con­sid­er vir­tue as what de­volves on him­self. He may not yield the per­for­mance of it e­ven to his teach­er.ʼ

15:36 The Mas­ter said, ʼThe su­pe­ri­or man is cor­rect­ly firm, and not firm mere­ly.ʼ

15:37 The Mas­ter said, ʼA min­is­ter, in serv­ing his prince, reverently dis­charges his duties, and makes his e­mol­u­ment a sec­ond­ar­y con­sid­er­a­tion.ʼ

15:38 The Mas­ter said, ʼIn teach­ing there should be no dis­tinc­tion of clas­ses.ʼ

15:39 The Mas­ter said, ʼThose whose cours­es are dif­fer­ent can­not lay plans for one an­oth­er.ʼ

15:40 The Mas­ter said, ʼIn lan­guage it is simp­ly re­quired that it con­vey the mean­ing.ʼ

15:41 The Mu­sic-master, Mien, hav­ing called up­on him, when they came to the steps, the Mas­ter said, ʼHere are the steps.ʼ When they came to the mat for the guest to sit up­on, he said, ʼHere is the mat.ʼ When all were seated, the Mas­ter in­formed him, say­ing, ʼSo and so is here; so and so is here.ʼ The Mu­sic-master, Mien, hav­ing gone out, Tsze-chang asked, say­ing. ʼIs it the rule to tell those things to the Mu­sic- mas­ter?ʼ The Mas­ter said, ʼYes. This is cer­tain­ly the rule for those who lead the blind.ʼ
